# win-loss — loss coding method (reference)

The method detail for cross-examining and coding closed-lost deals. Grounded in published win/loss practice (Clozd's driver framework and closed-lost-analysis failure modes; CRM-vs-buyer discrepancy research), adapted for agent execution over HubSpot.

## Why the recorded reason is a hypothesis

The published numbers, for calibration (do not re-quote as Oolio findings): rep-entered loss reasons run inaccurate in roughly 60% of cases; buyer-reported and CRM-recorded reasons match only about a third of the time; in one 1,000-deal study the competitor tagged in CRM was wrong in about 70% of cases. Reps reach for price, product, and timing because those explanations are safe. So: the recorded reason starts the investigation, never ends it.

## The cross-examination checklist (per deal)

Work through what the metadata says, against the recorded reason:

1. **Time-in-stage.** A deal that sat in one stage far beyond segment norm then closed lost usually stalled on something specific (approval, integration question, champion loss), whatever the tag says.
2. **Engagement drop-off.** When did replies/meetings stop, and what was the last substantive exchange about? The last real objection before silence is often the real reason.
3. **Competitor evidence.** Is a competitor actually named in notes, emails, or meeting summaries? A competitor tag with no mention anywhere in the record is unreliable; an unmentioned-in-tag competitor named in notes overrides the tag.
4. **Price context.** "Lost on price" with a deal size normal for the segment and no discount discussion in the record is suspect. Price given as reason + no negotiation attempt recorded usually means the value case failed, which codes as product fit or sales experience, not pricing.
5. **Timing claims.** "Bad timing" or "went dark" with healthy engagement until a specific event points at the event.

Verdict per deal: coded driver + **corroborated** (metadata supports it) or **rep-reported only** (nothing beyond the tag). Downweight rep-reported-only in patterns; never headline them.

## The four drivers

- **Product / solution fit** — a capability, integration, scalability, or platform gap; includes "their product genuinely fits this vertical better".
- **Sales experience** — responsiveness, demo quality, trust, procurement handling, champion management. The driver reps essentially never self-report.
- **Pricing / packaging** — genuine price/value mismatch, packaging misfit (forced bundles, contract terms), payment-processing economics. Only when the record shows price was actually engaged with, per checklist rule 4.
- **Competitive** — lost to a rival's specific play (incumbency, exclusivity, a killer feature, a buyout of remaining contract). Always also record which competitor; feed the dossier.

One primary driver per loss; a secondary where the record genuinely supports two. No "other" bucket — unclassifiable losses are listed with their evidence, and a pile of them means either the records are too thin (a finding about CRM hygiene) or the framework is missing something (a finding about us).

## Pattern thresholds

- Default: **5+ deals** on the same theme in the period before it reports as a pattern. Below that: "watch" with a count, carried to the next run.
- Segment-specific claims need the threshold met **within** the segment, not across the pool.
- Percentages need denominators stated (7 of 31 losses, not 23%).
- Compare each pattern with the prior run (Brain evidence log): new / growing / static / shrinking. Growth is the headline, not existence.

## Interview guide template (the human handover)

For each consequential loss selected, a half-page guide:

```
Deal: <name, segment, size, closed date> — coded <driver>, <corroborated | rep-reported only>
What the record says: 2-3 lines, with the specific gaps in our knowledge.
Ask (open, non-leading, in order):
1. Walk me through how the decision unfolded — what were the real turning points?
2. What did <winner> show you that mattered most?
3. What nearly made you choose us? What stopped that?
4. If our team could change one thing about how we ran this, what should it be?
5. <one deal-specific question targeting the corroboration gap>
Avoid: yes/no forms of the above; defending our product; the phrase "price" until they raise it.
Note: best conducted by someone not on the deal (politeness bias runs high with the rep who lost it).
```

## Failure modes to avoid (from the published practice)

Programmes die from: trusting rep-entered reasons; no executive owner; insights siloed from product and sales; the deal's own rep conducting interviews. The agent-side mitigations are built into the workflow (cross-examination, routing, guides); the ownership one belongs to the human, and the report should name a suggested owner if none exists.
