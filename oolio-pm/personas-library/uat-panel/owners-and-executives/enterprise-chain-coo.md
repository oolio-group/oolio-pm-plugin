# Caroline "Caz" Whitfield

> Runs 600 pubs on margins of pence. Has opinions.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Chief Operating Officer, Stagcoach Inns (a UK national pub group, ~620 venues)
- **Age**: 51
- **Location**: Watford, United Kingdom
- **Venue**: National. Head office above a pub in Watford. She is in a different region every week.
- **Reports to**: The CEO and the Board.
- **Manages**: A regional operations team of 14 (six regional directors, ops support, audit), in turn covering ~620 venues.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Enterprise chain, 600+ venues
- **Service style**: Pub and hotel, casual food, mass-market
- **Geography**: United Kingdom
- **Tech maturity**: Strategic. Has a CIO and an integration team. Has opinions on APIs.
- **Buying motion**: Procurement-led, with executive sponsorship

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: bepoz at venue level for new acquisitions and pilots. Group-wide POS contract is held by an incumbent. Oolio is in a competitive cycle here, not an installed-base story.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, payments, KDS, loyalty (membership scheme), stock and ordering, labour, BI, integrations with everything.

---

## Bio

Caz spent 14 years at a major UK pub group rising from Area Manager to Operations Director before being recruited to Stagcoach as COO five years ago. She has run a pub. She has run a region. She now runs an estate. She has done two POS roll-outs in her career and is fluent in why they fail.

She is not romantic about technology. She is romantic about throughput, wage cost, and consistency across 620 sites. She will spend an hour walking a venue in Hull before she signs off a vendor pitch in London.

She is the person procurement, IT, and finance defer to on operational fit. If she is not convinced, the deal is dead. If she is convinced, the deal still has to clear procurement, security, and a 90-day pilot.

---

## A day in the life

A Tuesday in the office.

- **07:30**: At her desk. Reads the overnight ops report. Two venues had POS issues during late trade. One was the third incident that month.
- **08:30**: Daily ops huddle with the regional directors. 12 minutes.
- **09:30**: Meets the CIO. They are reviewing a vendor RFP shortlist for the next POS contract. She wants to add a fourth name. He wants to remove one.
- **11:00**: Calls the GM of a flagship venue. Has a frank conversation about food margin. The GM has reasons. She has counter-reasons.
- **13:00**: Lunch at her desk while she reads a deck from a payments vendor. She makes three notes, all unflattering.
- **14:30**: Walks finance through next year's tech opex assumptions. Gets pushed back on. Pushes back on the push back.
- **16:30**: Drives to a venue in Hertfordshire for the early evening. Sits at the bar. Watches a POS take 14 seconds to load the menu after a tab is opened. Times it on her phone. Sends a screenshot to the CIO.
- **20:30**: Home. Replies to four emails. Reads a board paper. Sleeps.

## Goals

- Drive a one to two percentage point improvement in operating margin without losing service consistency.
- Cut wage cost percentage by 0.5 points through better forecasting and labour scheduling integrated with POS data.
- Standardise the operational experience across 620 venues so a manager moving from one venue to another can be productive on day one.
- Reduce IT cost per venue by consolidating overlapping systems.
- Make the next vendor decision the right one, because she will be the one held accountable for five years.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- The incumbent POS is fine. Fine is the problem. There is no commercial pressure to innovate and the platform is showing its age.
- Vendors pitch her group as if it were a multi-site operator. It is not. It is an estate. The difference is not understood.
- Integration is always the unspoken catch. Every vendor says "yes we integrate", almost nobody can demonstrate end to end with her actual stack.
- A pilot is expensive in pub time and venue time. A failed pilot is a leadership credibility cost.
- Reporting is fragmented across BI, POS, labour, and finance, and the numbers do not always agree.
- Front-line staff are casual, transient, and trained in 90 minutes. Anything that adds clicks to the till adds wage cost.

---

## KPIs she is judged on

- Operating margin, group level, quarterly.
- Wage cost as a percentage of revenue.
- Throughput per venue per hour during peak.
- Like-for-like sales versus prior year.
- Estate-wide POS and payments uptime, measured to four nines.
- Successful project delivery (vendor roll-outs, refurbishments) on time and on budget.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in her hand**: Corporate iPhone, Surface laptop, the venue POS when she is on site.
- **Current stack**: An incumbent enterprise POS, a separate payments processor, a labour platform, a centralised BI tool, a membership and loyalty scheme, a stock ordering tool, a learning management system for staff.
- **Comfort level**: Strategic, in the sense that she runs the stack with the CIO and is fluent in vendor commercials. She is not technical.
- **What she will not tolerate**: Pilot results that do not survive production. Vendors who go around her to the CEO. Anything that adds friction at the till.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Operational decisions and pilots up to a defined threshold. Group POS, payments, and platform decisions are joint with the CIO and approved by the Board.
- **Influences**: Everything operational. She is the executive sponsor that procurement and IT need to move.
- **Cannot touch**: Brand-level marketing and capital allocation decisions, in isolation.
- **Who she needs on side**: CIO, CFO, the senior regional directors, the venue GMs at pilot sites.

> "Your platform is interesting. Walk me through how it survives a Saturday in Sheffield."

> "If you sell us as if we are five venues, you will lose. If you understand we are 620 venues, you might win."

---

## What she needs from Oolio

- Demonstrable enterprise-grade reliability. Real uptime numbers, real incident reviews, real estate-scale references.
- A platform story, not five products. If Oolio cannot tell her how the brand portfolio resolves into one decision for her, she will not engage.
- An integration story that survives her CIO's first technical session.
- A pilot model that is honest about cost and timeline and that protects venue revenue.
- Operational benchmarking she cannot get elsewhere. Cross-estate cost lines, throughput patterns, labour ratios. This is where Oolio's consultancy proposition is most valuable to her.
- A named executive sponsor at Oolio. Not the salesperson.

---

## What loses her

- A pitch that mentions "all-in-one" without showing how it integrates with what she already has.
- A vendor who cannot produce another enterprise UK reference at her scale.
- A pilot in a single venue with no plan for scale.
- Anything that smells like a startup playbook.
- A salesperson going around her.

---

## Strategic signal

Caz is the persona that decides whether Oolio's stated ambition to be a hospitality technology partner is credible at the top of the market. The enterprise UK pub segment is the proof point. Winning Caz means winning estate-scale references and the consultancy revenue that comes with them. Losing her means Oolio stays a sub-enterprise brand. Every product, GTM, and integration decision that touches the enterprise pub vertical should be tested against this persona before it is committed.
