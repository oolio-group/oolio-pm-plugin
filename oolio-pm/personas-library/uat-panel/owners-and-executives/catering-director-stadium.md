# Michael "Mick" Torrance

> Owns the stadium's hospitality P&L. Judged event by event.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a contract caterer's hospitality director at a major Australian stadium to represent the stadia and events executive. Mick, the venue, and the caterer are invented. The pattern is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Catering Director, Harbourfield Stadium contract, Meridian Catering Group (a national contract caterer)
- **Age**: 48
- **Location**: Melbourne, Australia
- **Venue**: The same stadium Dani Hartigan runs operationally. He owns the whole hospitality business inside it: around 240 concourse points of sale, 68 corporate boxes and suites, function spaces, and the members' dining rooms.
- **Reports to**: Meridian's state Managing Director, and commercially to the stadium's own management as the client.
- **Manages**: Dani (venue operations), a corporate hospitality manager, an executive chef, a commercial and reporting analyst, and a finance partner.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Enterprise (national contract caterer; he runs one flagship contract inside a multi-venue portfolio)
- **Service style**: Stadia, events, convention
- **Geography**: Australia, Melbourne
- **Tech maturity**: Deliberate. He does not configure systems, but he chooses them, and he has been burnt enough to ask hard questions.
- **Buying motion**: Procurement-led (Meridian head office contracts the platform; he is the executive sponsor whose contract proves or breaks it)

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: swiftpos across the precinct, as the stadia and events platform. The decision would be made at caterer head office with his sponsorship.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, payments, self-ordering, stock, member and loyalty pricing, and above all the reporting layer across all of it.

## Bio

Mick has spent 25 years in event hospitality: hotel banqueting, then a convention centre, then stadium catering, first as an operations manager and for the last seven years running contracts. He won the Harbourfield contract for Meridian at its last tender and has to defend it at the next one. That fact shapes everything he does.

His business is unlike a restaurant group's. Revenue arrives in violent spikes, forty-odd times a year, and each event is its own miniature P&L: its own crowd, menu, pricing, staffing bill, and stock burn. His two worlds have different economics. The general concourse is a volume machine measured in spend per head and speed of service. Corporate boxes and members' dining are a premium machine measured in package yield and the renewal rate of the companies that buy them. Sitting across both are obligations he did not choose: the stadium's pouring-rights sponsor dictates what beer is poured, and his contract dictates what he reports to the venue and when. The morning after an event, his numbers need to be on two desks, Meridian's and the stadium's, and they need to agree.

## A day in the life

The Sunday morning after a 49,000-crowd Saturday night match, then a working week.

- **07:00**: At his desk at the stadium. Pulls the event flash: revenue by outlet group, spend per head, labour cost against plan.
- **08:30**: His analyst walks him through the exceptions. Two concourse stands underperformed. A corporate box was invoiced the wrong beverage package. The mid-strength beer sold out in one stand, which is a spend problem and a sponsor conversation.
- **10:00**: Writes the event summary for the stadium client and Meridian state MD. He wants it sent before midday while the event is fresh.
- **13:00**: Reviews the labour bill. Nine hundred casuals were rostered; the actual cost landed 3% over plan. He wants to know which zones.
- **Monday 09:00**: Weekly with Dani and the executive chef. Next event is Tuesday's concert: different crowd, different menu, different pricing, higher spend per head expected.
- **Monday 11:00**: Pouring-rights compliance review with the sponsor's account manager. Volumes by brand, by outlet, from the POS data.
- **Monday 14:00**: Corporate hospitality pipeline: box renewals for next season, package pricing, two prospects wanting function-space quotes.
- **Wednesday**: Monthly contract review with the stadium's commercial director. His numbers face the client's numbers. They must reconcile.
- **Friday**: Meridian portfolio call. His contract's month against the other venues in the state. Benchmarks matter here.

## Goals

- Grow revenue per event, spend per head on the concourse and package yield in corporate, season on season.
- Hold labour cost per event inside plan without starving the burst windows.
- Retain the contract at the next tender, which means a happy stadium client and clean, credible reporting.
- Renew the corporate box holders, the highest-margin revenue he has.
- Meet every sponsor and contractual obligation with evidence, not assertion.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- Per-event reconciliation is an industry unto itself: hundreds of tills, hundreds of casual operators, multiple price tiers, corporate packages, and it all has to close by the next morning.
- Concourse and corporate are one business on two different logics, and most systems report them as if they were the same thing.
- Labour is his biggest controllable cost and his data on it arrives after the event, when nothing can be done.
- Sponsor pourage reporting is assembled by hand from POS extracts every month. An error is a commercial incident.
- The stadium client sees his numbers. A discrepancy between systems is not an IT issue, it is a trust issue at the next contract review.
- A mid-event system failure lands on his desk as lost revenue, a client complaint, and a tender-day story, all at once.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Contract profit, per event and per season.
- Spend per head, per event, versus target and versus prior comparable events.
- Labour cost per event as a percentage of event revenue.
- Corporate hospitality yield and box renewal rate.
- Client satisfaction and contract retention.
- Sponsor and contractual compliance, evidenced.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: Laptop and phone. On event nights, a live sales dashboard, and a walk of the concourse and the corporate level at peak.
- **Current stack**: The venue POS and payments platform, a stock system, a casual workforce platform, corporate hospitality booking and invoicing, Meridian's finance system, and a reporting layer that his analyst holds together across all of them.
- **Comfort level**: Deliberate. He consumes reporting hard and daily, and evaluates systems on whether they shorten the morning after.
- **What he will not tolerate**: Numbers that do not reconcile, reporting that arrives late, and any platform risk that could surface mid-event in front of his client.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Event pricing within contract terms, staffing plans, local suppliers within Meridian's framework, corporate package structures.
- **Influences**: Meridian's platform decisions decisively. Head office will not impose a POS on a flagship contract over its director's objection, and a win at his venue becomes the portfolio reference.
- **Cannot touch**: Meridian's group procurement contracts, the stadium's sponsorship deals, the venue's own capital works.
- **Who he needs on side**: The stadium's commercial director (the client), Meridian's state MD, Dani, and his analyst.

## What he needs from Oolio

- Per-event reporting that closes overnight: revenue, labour, and stock by outlet, credible enough to hand straight to the client.
- One platform view across concourse volume and corporate hospitality, with the economics of each kept distinct.
- Live labour and sales during the event, so cost decisions can be made mid-shift, not post-mortem.
- Sponsor and pourage reporting straight from the transaction data, not from spreadsheets.
- Reliability he can put his name against in front of a client and, eventually, a tender panel.
- Benchmarking across events and, ideally, across venues. He runs a portfolio-shaped business and thinks in comparisons.

---

## What loses him

- A reconciliation gap. One event of numbers that do not add up costs more trust than a year of features earns.
- Reporting built for restaurants with the event bolted on.
- Any mid-event failure handled slowly or quietly.
- A pitch about the concourse that has no answer for corporate boxes, functions, and members' dining.
- A vendor who does not understand that his client and his sponsor read his data too.

---

## Strategic signal

Mick is the buyer-side proof of the stadia and events vertical. swiftpos's market is decided by executives like him: contract caterers who run portfolios of venues and choose platforms per contract, then standardise on what wins. He is also where Oolio's partner-and-expertise ambition gets tested hardest, because what he buys is not a till, it is the credibility of his numbers in front of a client and a tender panel. Win Mick's contract review and the platform inherits a portfolio. Lose one reconciliation and it exits at the next tender.
