# Damien O'Brien

> Built four bars from one. Now trying to run them like one.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Founder and Managing Director, Iron Lane Group
- **Age**: 47
- **Location**: Sydney, Australia
- **Venue**: Iron Lane Group. Four venues: a cocktail bar (Surry Hills), a wine bar (Newtown), a pub (Marrickville), a small restaurant (Bondi Junction).
- **Reports to**: His business partner and his bank.
- **Manages**: A four-person head office (himself, an operations manager, a part-time bookkeeper, a marketing freelancer) plus four venue GMs.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Small group, 4 venues
- **Service style**: Mixed. Bars, pub, casual dining.
- **Geography**: Australia, Sydney
- **Tech maturity**: Deliberate, trending Strategic
- **Buying motion**: Founder-and-ops

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Mixed, painfully. Two venues on ordermate, one on idealpos (legacy), one on bepoz (acquired with the pub). Consolidation is a live project.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, Payments, Loyalty, online ordering, KDS in two venues, stock control in two venues.

---

## Bio

Damien started behind a bar at 19. He bought into a single Surry Hills cocktail bar at 31, bought his partner out two years later, and acquired or opened three more venues over the following decade. He is no longer behind any of the bars. He spends most of his time in a small office above the Surry Hills venue, on a laptop, talking to GMs.

He hired Sarah, his operations manager, two years ago. That was the moment the business shifted from "four venues I run" to "a group". Sarah is now systematising. Damien still thinks like a publican. The friction between the two is productive.

He is now thinking about venue five. He is also thinking about whether venue one is still pulling its weight. He cannot answer either question with the data he has.

## A day in the life

- **08:30**: Coffee at the Surry Hills bar. Reads last night's takings from all four venues on his phone. Iron Lane Marrickville is down 14% on the comparable Friday. Texts the GM.
- **10:00**: Calls Sarah. Goes over the supplier consolidation project. Three venues use the same beer wholesaler, one uses a different one because the GM has a relationship.
- **11:30**: Walks the floor at Surry Hills. Talks to the head bartender. The new POS at the wine bar is slow at peak and the staff are complaining.
- **13:00**: Sits with a candidate for the Bondi Junction GM role. Three interviews this week.
- **15:00**: Reviews a P&L pack from his accountant. The numbers do not match what the POS says. Spends 45 minutes trying to reconcile.
- **17:00**: Site visit to Newtown. Walks in unannounced. Watches a shift change. Has a quiet drink at the bar to feel the room.
- **20:00**: Home. Replies to a WhatsApp from a competitor who wants to sell their venue. Considers it for ten minutes. Replies "send me the numbers".

## Goals

- A single view of the four venues that he trusts.
- Standardised operations across venues without losing the local personality of each.
- A clear answer on which venue to invest in next, which to fix, which to sell.
- Time to think strategically. Currently he is buried in operational noise.
- A path to ten venues without the cost line doubling.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- Four POS systems, three payments providers, two stock systems, one Xero. He has to live in spreadsheets to see the group.
- Two of the four venues are still running on the system they had when he bought them. Migration is scary and expensive.
- Reports are per-venue. Group-level reporting is something Sarah builds in Google Sheets every Tuesday.
- Vendors sell to him at venue level. Nobody sells him a group.
- His GMs each have a favourite system and resent change.
- Payments fees vary across venues for no reason he can defend.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Group revenue and group EBITDA, monthly.
- Wage cost as a percentage of revenue per venue.
- Cost of goods sold per category per venue, benchmarked across the group.
- Cash position and lender covenants.
- His own ratio of office time to floor time. He tracks this himself.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: iPhone, MacBook Pro, occasionally an iPad if he is on site.
- **Current stack**: Mixed POS across the group, Tyro and a bank EFTPOS at different venues, Xero, Deputy, MarketMan in two venues, a homegrown reporting workbook.
- **Comfort level**: Deliberate. He understands what he wants from systems. He does not understand the underlying technology and does not want to.
- **What he will not tolerate**: Being sold a "group product" that is actually four single-venue products with a logo on top.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Everything, within reason. Group-level decisions are his.
- **Influences**: His four GMs, who can each kill a rollout by being passively unhelpful.
- **Cannot touch**: GM-level autonomy he has explicitly granted (menu, local hires, local marketing).
- **Who he needs on side**: Sarah (ops manager), the two GMs whose venues are on the legacy system.

## What he needs from Oolio

- A single contract, a single account manager, a single bill, across all venues he chooses to put on Oolio.
- A migration plan with a named owner, not a quote.
- Group-level reporting that Sarah does not have to rebuild.
- Honest answers when he asks "should we open venue five or fix venue one". That is the consultancy proposition.
- Benchmarks. He wants to know how his Marrickville pub compares to other pubs of the same size on Oolio. Without that, the data is just his own data.

---

## What loses him

- Three siloed implementations dressed up as a group offer.
- A salesperson who treats him like he is buying his first POS.
- A migration that takes a venue down on a trading night.
- A loyalty product that does not work across his four venues.
- Being told "that is a roadmap item".

---

## Strategic signal

Damien is the persona who validates whether Oolio can move from selling tills to selling a platform. He is at the inflection point where he is consolidating vendors and asking strategic questions. If Oolio can win him at the group level (one contract, one platform, real cross-venue intelligence, advisory bundled in) Oolio has a real growth story in the small-group segment. If Oolio sells to him venue by venue, a competitor will sell him a group.
