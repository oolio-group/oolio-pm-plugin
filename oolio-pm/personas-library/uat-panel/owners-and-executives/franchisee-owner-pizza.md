# Nick Castellano

> Three Domino's stores. Lives and dies by delivery time.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a Domino's franchisee to represent the takeaway and pizza vertical. Nick is an invented individual. The brand is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Franchisee, Castellano Pizza Co (operates three Domino's stores under franchise)
- **Age**: 44
- **Location**: Gold Coast, Queensland, Australia
- **Venue**: Three Domino's stores across the northern Gold Coast. Delivery and pickup only, no dine-in. Long hours, heavy weekend and late-night trade.
- **Reports to**: The franchisor. He owns the stores but operates inside the brand's systems, menu, and online ordering.
- **Manages**: Three store managers directly, and through them around 90 team members and drivers.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Small group, 3 stores, franchisee of a pizza and takeaway brand
- **Service style**: Takeaway and pizza
- **Geography**: Australia, Gold Coast
- **Tech maturity**: Deliberate. The brand is tech-forward and he leans into it, watching the data on every store.
- **Buying motion**: Franchisor-mandated. Online ordering, POS, and dispatch are set centrally by the brand. He optimises within them and buys only where the brand leaves a gap.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits deliverit (online ordering, delivery dispatch, kitchen flow) with the OOLIO platform for in-store POS and KDS. In reality his core systems are brand-mandated, so this is a fit assessment.
- **Solutions they touch**: Online ordering, POS, KDS and makeline, delivery dispatch and tracking, aggregators, payments, labour, and brand reporting.

## Bio

Nick bought his first Domino's franchise fifteen years ago and built up to three stores by running them hard and watching every number. His business is volume and speed. A pizza is a few dollars of margin, so the money is made in throughput, in delivery times, and in keeping labour tight without slowing the make.

He respects the brand precisely because it is good at technology. Online ordering, tracking, and the data behind it are why the model works. What he owns is execution across three stores: getting the makeline moving, getting drivers out the door, and keeping team members long enough to be worth training. He is not romantic about it. He looks at delivery time and labour percentage, and he acts.

---

## A day in the life

A Friday, the biggest night of the week.

- **10:00**: Reviews last night across all three stores. One store's average delivery time crept up. He wants to know why.
- **11:00**: Calls that store's manager. The makeline was backing up at peak and two drivers were late on.
- **13:00**: Walks a store. Watches the makeline screen and the dispatch board through a lunch rush.
- **15:00**: Reviews the driver roster against the forecast for the night. Friday needs every car on the road.
- **17:30**: At his busiest store as the dinner surge builds. Stands by the cut bench watching order flow from screen to box to driver.
- **19:00**: Peak. Online orders pouring in, makeline full, drivers cycling in and out. He watches the dispatch times tick.
- **20:30**: A store's oven goes down briefly. He is on the phone managing the diversion of orders.
- **22:00**: Late trade. Checks the night's delivery times and labour across all three before he heads home.

---

## Goals

- Keep average delivery time down across all three stores, because it drives repeat orders and reviews.
- Protect thin per-order margin by running labour tight without slowing the make or the dispatch.
- Reduce team member and driver turnover, since both are constant training cost.
- Get one clear, comparable view of all three stores, not three separate dashboards.
- Roll out any brand change across three stores without losing a busy night.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- A makeline or dispatch slow-down at peak hits delivery time, which hits repeat orders directly.
- Drivers are casual and turn over fast, and a short driver roster on a Friday is lost sales.
- Aggregator orders arrive alongside his own online orders and complicate the kitchen flow.
- He carries the cost of systems set centrally, and any failure is still his problem to manage live.
- Reporting across three stores takes assembling, when he wants one view.
- Late-night trade is where errors and labour creep both happen.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Average delivery time, per store and group.
- Order accuracy and remake rate.
- Labour cost as a percentage of sales.
- Online ordering conversion and repeat-order rates.
- Sales growth across the three stores.
- Driver availability and safety compliance.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: Phone, laptop, and the store systems when on site.
- **Current stack**: Brand online ordering, POS, KDS and makeline screens, dispatch and GPS tracking, aggregator tablets, payments, labour and scheduling, brand reporting.
- **Comfort level**: Deliberate. He understands the model is built on technology and he reads the data daily.
- **What he will not tolerate**: Anything that slows the makeline or dispatch at peak. A rollout that costs him a Friday.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Anything inside his group that the brand leaves to the franchisee, including labour tools, local capital, and gap-filling systems.
- **Influences**: The brand, through franchisee channels and his store results.
- **Cannot touch**: The online ordering platform, the menu, pricing, and brand marketing.
- **Who he needs on side**: His store managers, his drivers, and the franchisor for anything platform-level.

## What they need from Oolio

- A real grasp that in franchise pizza the brand is the buyer and the franchisee runs the throughput.
- Anything that keeps the makeline and dispatch moving at peak.
- One comparable view across all three stores.
- Order flow that brings online, phone, and aggregator into one clean kitchen view.
- A rollout model that never costs a trading night.

---

## What loses them

- A pitch that assumes he can swap his core online ordering or POS. He cannot.
- Anything that adds a step to the makeline or the dispatch.
- A vendor who does not understand the franchisor and franchisee split.
- A change that lands without a plan for three live, busy stores.

---

## Strategic signal

Nick shows Oolio's path into the takeaway and pizza vertical, and it mirrors QSR: the brand holds the buying power, the franchisee carries the operation. The route in is through the brand or master-franchisor, with franchisee delivery-time and labour results as the proof. As a three-store operator he also anchors the small-group end of this vertical. Every delivery, dispatch, online ordering, and KDS decision for pizza should be tested against Nick and the brand above him.
