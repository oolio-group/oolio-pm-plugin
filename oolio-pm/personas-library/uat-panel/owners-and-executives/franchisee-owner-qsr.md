# Raymond "Ray" Tan

> Runs nine McDonald's restaurants. The brand sets the rules.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a McDonald's franchisee to represent the QSR vertical. Ray is an invented individual. The brand is used as the operating context, not as an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Franchisee and Managing Director, Tan Restaurant Group (operates nine McDonald's restaurants under franchise)
- **Age**: 53
- **Location**: Greater Western Sydney, Australia
- **Venue**: Nine McDonald's restaurants across Western Sydney, a mix of drive-thru, McCafé, and shopping-centre sites. Head office is a small unit above one of the restaurants.
- **Reports to**: The franchisor. He owns the business but operates inside the brand's standards, supply chain, and systems.
- **Manages**: A small group office (operations manager, a finance and payroll lead, a people and training lead) and, through them, nine restaurant managers and roughly 650 crew.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Mid-market group, 9 restaurants, franchisee of a global QSR brand
- **Service style**: QSR
- **Geography**: Australia, Sydney
- **Tech maturity**: Strategic in ambition, constrained in practice. He runs a data-led business on systems the brand mandates.
- **Buying motion**: Franchisor-mandated. The brand sets the platform. Ray influences within brand-approved options, and chooses freely only where the brand leaves a gap.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits the OOLIO platform (self-ordering kiosks, KDS, payments) with deliverit for delivery and aggregator flow. In reality a McDonald's franchisee runs brand-mandated systems, so this is a fit assessment, not an installed-base story.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, self-ordering kiosks, KDS, payments, drive-thru, delivery integrations, labour, and brand reporting.

## Bio

Ray took on his first McDonald's restaurant twenty years ago after a career in retail management. He now operates nine. He did not build a brand, he bought into one, and he has made his money by running it tighter and cleaner than the franchisee down the road. He thinks in percentages, in seconds per car, and in crew retention.

He is proud of the business and clear-eyed about the deal. The brand owns the customer, the menu, the marketing, and most of the technology. What Ray owns is execution: labour, throughput, cleanliness, local people, and the bottom line across nine sites. He is not waiting for a vendor to change his life. He is looking for anything, inside the rules, that shaves cost or wins back a few seconds at peak.

## A day in the life

A Thursday across the group.

- **06:30**: Reads the overnight numbers across all nine restaurants. One drive-thru missed its speed-of-service target three days running.
- **07:30**: Calls the manager of that restaurant. Not a telling-off, a what-is-actually-happening conversation. A kiosk and a KDS bump bar are both playing up.
- **09:00**: Meets his operations manager. They walk the week's labour against forecast sales. Two sites are over.
- **10:30**: Sits in on a supplier and brand systems update call. A new payments requirement is coming. He has questions about the cutover.
- **12:00**: Drives to a shopping-centre restaurant at lunch peak. Stands by the kiosks. Watches how many customers abandon a slow screen and walk to the counter.
- **14:00**: Reviews a capital request to refit a restaurant's kitchen and digital order points. Margin per site has to justify it.
- **16:00**: People meeting. Crew turnover is the number that keeps him up. Training time is wage cost.
- **18:30**: Home, but watching the evening trade on his phone across all nine.

---

## Goals

- Hold or improve restaurant margin across all nine sites without breaching a single brand standard.
- Cut speed of service at the drive-thru and the kiosks during peak, because seconds are sales.
- Reduce crew turnover, because every leaver is training cost and a slower shift.
- Get clean, comparable numbers across all nine restaurants in one place, not nine versions of the truth.
- Make any technology change land without disrupting a single trading day.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- He carries the cost and the risk of systems he did not choose and cannot replace.
- A change mandated by the brand still has to be rolled out by him, across nine sites, without losing trade.
- Kiosks and KDS that lag at peak cost him money in the exact minutes that matter most.
- Reporting comes from several systems that do not always agree, so he spends time reconciling instead of deciding.
- Crew are young, casual, and turn over fast. Anything that needs real training does not scale.
- Delivery through aggregators is margin-thin and operationally messy, but he cannot ignore it.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Restaurant margin, per site and group level.
- Speed of service at drive-thru and kiosk, measured in seconds.
- Crew turnover and labour cost as a percentage of sales.
- Order accuracy and complaint rates.
- Sales growth like-for-like across the nine restaurants.
- Compliance with brand operational and food-safety standards.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: iPhone, laptop, and the restaurant systems when he is on site.
- **Current stack**: Brand-mandated POS, self-ordering kiosks, KDS, drive-thru systems, payments, a labour and scheduling tool, brand reporting, and delivery aggregator tablets.
- **Comfort level**: Strategic. He runs a multi-site business on data and is fluent in what each system costs him, even where he cannot change it.
- **What he will not tolerate**: Latency at peak. A rollout that drops a trading day. Reporting he has to reconcile by hand.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Anything inside his own group that the brand leaves to the franchisee, including labour tools, local capital, and gap-filling systems.
- **Influences**: The brand, as one of many franchisees, through franchisee forums and his operational results.
- **Cannot touch**: The mandated platform, the menu, the marketing, the supply chain. These are the franchisor's.
- **Who he needs on side**: His operations manager, his restaurant managers, and, for anything platform-level, the franchisor.

## Quotes

> "Show me seconds at the drive-thru or dollars on the margin. Everything else is a slide."

> "If a rollout costs me a trading day, it has already failed."

---

## What they need from Oolio

- An honest understanding that in franchise QSR the brand is the buyer, and the franchisee is the operator who carries the change.
- Anything, inside brand rules, that takes seconds out of peak service at the kiosk, counter, or drive-thru.
- Reporting that gives one comparable view across all nine restaurants.
- A rollout model that never costs a trading day.
- Tools a casual crew member can use with almost no training.

---

## What loses them

- A pitch that assumes he can simply switch his core platform. He cannot.
- Anything that adds clicks or latency at peak.
- A vendor who does not understand the franchisor and franchisee split.
- A rollout plan that ignores the cost of disruption across nine live restaurants.

---

## Strategic signal

Ray is the persona that shows how Oolio actually wins in QSR. The individual franchisee feels the pain, but the buying power sits with the brand. Oolio's route into this vertical is through the franchisor or master-franchisor, with the franchisee's operational results as the evidence. Ray also begins to fill the mid-market gap in the library: a nine-site operator with a real head office, sitting between the small group owner and the enterprise estate. Every QSR product and GTM decision should be tested against both Ray and the brand above him.
