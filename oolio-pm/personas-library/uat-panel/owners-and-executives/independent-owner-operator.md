# Amelia "Mel" Sutton

> Owns the cafe, runs the cafe, is the cafe.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Owner and operator, Stone and Sparrow
- **Age**: 38
- **Location**: Brunswick, Melbourne, Australia
- **Venue**: Stone and Sparrow. A 48-seat specialty cafe, breakfast and lunch trade, takeaway counter, small wholesale coffee account on the side.
- **Reports to**: The bank, the landlord, and her accountant.
- **Manages**: A floating team of 11. One full-time chef, one full-time barista, the rest casuals.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Independent, 1 venue
- **Service style**: Cafe
- **Geography**: Australia, Melbourne
- **Tech maturity**: Deliberate
- **Buying motion**: Owner-led

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: idealpos or the OOLIO platform
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, Payments, Loyalty, online ordering, third-party delivery integrations

## Bio

Mel opened Stone and Sparrow seven years ago after eight years working her way up through Melbourne's specialty coffee scene. She did the dishies-to-floor-manager route at three of the best-known cafes in the city before borrowing against her parents' house and taking a shopfront on Sydney Road that everyone said was too far north.

She is now in year seven, in profit, just. She is on the floor four days a week and in the back office two nights a week. She does the rosters, the orders, the marketing, and most of the hiring. She did the BAS herself until last year. The cafe is her income, her identity, and her loan repayment plan all at once.

---

## A day in the life

A regular Friday.

- **05:50**: Walks in, fires the espresso machine, checks the dough proof from overnight, scrolls last night's takings on her phone before the team arrives.
- **06:30**: Opens. Two staff. Coffee orders start.
- **08:30**: Peak rush. She is on bar. A bank deposit needs to be done, a kitchen porter has called in sick, a third-party delivery order is sitting on the printer because the screen has frozen.
- **10:30**: Brunch slows. She fixes the printer by power-cycling the dock, again. Sits with a coffee and replies to four DMs from suppliers.
- **12:30**: Lunch peak. She is now expediting because the chef is in the weeds.
- **15:00**: Trade slows. She runs the day's report on the POS, flags a void that does not look right, has a quiet word with the staff member who did it.
- **16:30**: Closes. Counts cash. Approves tomorrow's roster on her phone in the car park.
- **20:00**: At home. Does the weekly stock order from her laptop. Reads a margin report from the POS that she half trusts and half does not.

---

## Goals

- Keep the cafe profitable enough to start paying herself properly.
- Open a second site within three years, without losing what makes Stone and Sparrow itself.
- Stop being the single point of failure on a Friday.
- Have a real day off without checking the POS app twice.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- The POS works most of the time. The 5% of the time it does not, it is during rush, and there is nothing she can do.
- Reports give her data. They do not give her answers. She has to do the maths in her head about whether last Tuesday was actually good.
- Every supplier wants to integrate. None of the integrations talk to each other. She types the same numbers into three systems.
- Third-party delivery apps eat her margin and her brand and she has no way to push back at the venue level.
- Hiring and training a new casual on the till takes a full shift she does not have.
- Payments fees are the second-biggest line item after rent and wages and she barely understands them.

---

## KPIs she is judged on

- Weekly revenue and weekly wage cost as a percentage of revenue.
- Coffee cost as a percentage of coffee revenue (a metric she tracks obsessively).
- Customer return rate, measured by gut feel and a half-finished loyalty list.
- Whether she paid herself this month.

## Tech profile

- **Devices in her hand**: iPhone (her primary management surface), a MacBook at home, two POS terminals on the counter, a kitchen printer, an EFTPOS device, an iPad for the manager.
- **Current stack**: A POS in the OOLIO family, EFTPOS terminal from her bank, Xero for accounting, Deputy for rostering, an Instagram account she manages herself, a delivery aggregator she is unhappy with.
- **Comfort level**: Deliberate. She has changed POS once before. She will read a release note. She will not read a 40-page admin guide.
- **What she will not tolerate**: Downtime during service. Hidden payment fees. Support that asks her to email logs.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Everything. It is her cafe and her credit card.
- **Influences**: The independent cafe peer group she runs in. If she switches, four others ask why.
- **Cannot touch**: Nothing internally. Externally she is constrained by the bank, the landlord, and what her chef will tolerate.
- **Who she needs on side**: Her chef. Her morning barista. If those two reject a system, the system goes.

## What she needs from Oolio

- A POS that does not fall over during rush, and a support number that picks up when it does.
- Payments that are obviously cheaper, not "competitive".
- A view of the business that tells her what to do, not just what happened.
- One person at Oolio who knows her name and her venue.
- Honest advice when she asks "should I open a second site". She would actually take it.

---

## What loses her

- Three downtime events in a month.
- A surprise fee on a payments invoice.
- A salesperson who tries to upsell her into modules she does not need.
- An onboarding process that takes her off the floor for two days.
- Being told to "raise a ticket".

---

## Strategic signal

Mel is the persona Oolio's brand purpose is written for. She is also the persona who tests whether Oolio's stated move from "POS company" to "hospitality partner that helps clients grow" is real. If Oolio can be the partner that helps her open a profitable second site, the consultancy proposition is true. If Oolio is just the till behind her counter, it is not.
