# Pavel Novak

> Twenty-four, own car, paid by the run. The app is his shift.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a Domino's delivery driver to represent last-mile delivery in the takeaway and pizza vertical. Pavel is an invented individual. The brand is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Delivery driver (casual, hourly plus per-delivery). Uses his own car
- **Age**: 24
- **Location**: Gold Coast, Queensland, Australia
- **Venue**: The same Domino's store Mia Roberts manages, in Nick Castellano's franchise group. He is on the road far more than in the store
- **Reports to**: The shift runner and store manager who dispatch him.
- **Manages**: Nobody.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Small group, 1 of 3 stores, franchised pizza brand
- **Service style**: Takeaway and pizza (last-mile delivery)
- **Geography**: Australia, Gold Coast
- **Tech maturity**: Reactive. He uses the dispatch and navigation on the device he is handed and his own phone. He wants it simple and one-handed.
- **Buying motion**: None. He is a user, never a buyer.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits the deliverit dispatch, tracking, and proof-of-delivery flow that a pizza driver works to. This is a fit assessment for last-mile delivery.
- **Solutions they touch**: Dispatch and run assignment, GPS tracking and navigation, proof of delivery, payments on delivery, the customer tracker that sets expectations.

## Bio

Pavel drives for the pizza store around his other commitments. He uses his own car, knows the suburbs around the store well, and is paid in a way that rewards getting runs done cleanly and quickly. His whole shift is a loop: take the sealed orders, see where they are going, drive the run, hand them over, take payment if it is owed, and get back for the next load.

He does not think about software. He thinks about the next address and the fastest safe way there. The dispatch and navigation are the tools that make or break his night. When the run is assigned clearly, the addresses are right, and the customer can see he is coming, it is smooth. When an address is wrong, an order is missing from the run, or payment at the door is fiddly, he is standing on a doorstep losing time he is not paid for, while the pizzas in the bag go cold.

---

## A day in the life

A Friday night on the road.

- **17:30**: Arrives, checks in, confirms his car and bag are ready. First runs already building.
- **18:00**: Takes his first load. Two orders, two addresses. Checks the run on the device before he pulls out.
- **18:30**: Hands over the first order, takes a card payment at the door, moves to the second.
- **19:15**: Peak. Loads of three and four orders. He sequences them by the map to keep them hot.
- **19:40**: An address comes up wrong. He calls the store, they check, he reroutes, but he has lost ten minutes.
- **20:30**: A customer is watching the tracker and meets him at the gate. Quick, clean handover.
- **21:30**: A run where one order was not in the bag. Back to the store, awkward with the customer, time lost.
- **23:00**: Last runs of the night, quieter roads, steady loop, then clocks off.

---

## Goals

- Get each run done cleanly and quickly, because clean runs are the whole job.
- Have the right orders, the right addresses, and clear navigation every time he leaves.
- Take payment at the door without fuss.
- Keep deliveries hot and customers happy, which keeps ratings up and shifts coming.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- A wrong or unclear address leaves him stuck on the road, unpaid for the time and with cold food.
- An order missing from his run means a trip back and an awkward customer.
- Fiddly payment at the door, a slow or unclear card flow, costs time at every stop.
- If the customer cannot see he is coming, he gets calls and meets closed doors.
- Doing anything on the device while managing a car, a bag, and a doorstep has to be one-handed and simple.
- Per-run pay means every minute lost to the system is money out of his pocket.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Not formally measured as an individual, but felt through:
- Delivery time and how hot the food arrives.
- Customer ratings and complaints on his deliveries.
- Runs completed per shift.
- Reliability and safety, which decide whether he keeps getting shifts.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: The dispatch and navigation device, his own phone, the payment terminal at the door. A car, a bag, and a doorbell, all at once.
- **Current stack**: Whatever the store runs for dispatch, tracking, and payments. He neither knows nor cares what it is called.
- **Comfort level**: Reactive, but quick with anything simple and one-handed. Maps and tap-to-pay he uses without thinking.
- **What he will not tolerate**: A wrong address, a fiddly payment, or anything that needs two hands and full attention on a doorstep.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Nothing.
- **Influences**: Nothing directly. His night is the truest test of whether dispatch, addresses, tracking, and door payment actually work.
- **Cannot touch**: Everything is set by the brand, the franchisee, and the store.
- **Who he needs on side**: The shift runner who dispatches him and sorts problems when an address or order is wrong.

## What they need from Oolio

- Runs assigned clearly, with the right orders and correct, navigable addresses every time.
- A one-handed flow that works on a doorstep, including payment.
- Customer tracking that sets expectations so people meet him at the door.
- A fast way to flag a wrong address or a missing order back to the store.

---

## What loses them

- Wrong or unclear addresses on a run.
- Orders that do not match what is in the bag.
- A slow or confusing payment flow at the door.
- Anything that needs two hands and full focus while he is mid-delivery.

---

## Strategic signal

Pavel is the last-mile reality of the takeaway and pizza vertical, the persona no in-store view captures. Delivery time, the number the franchisee and store manager live by, is realised on his runs, and the customer's final impression of the whole order is his handover at the door. He is the test for every dispatch, tracking, address, and proof-of-delivery decision. A delivery flow that is clean in the store but breaks on his doorstep has failed where it matters most. He also marks the edge of the current role grouping: a customer-facing last-mile role that sits in front of house but is almost never in the house.
