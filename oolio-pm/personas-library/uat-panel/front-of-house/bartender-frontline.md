# Jess Carmody

> Behind the bar three nights a week. Touches the till 800 times a shift.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Bartender (casual, hourly)
- **Age**: 23
- **Location**: Brisbane, Australia
- **Venue**: A 200-cover suburban pub with a busy sports trade. Part of a small group of four venues.
- **Reports to**: The bar manager and, in their absence, the shift leader.
- **Manages**: Nobody. Trains new starters informally.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Small group, 1 of 4 venues
- **Service style**: Pub
- **Geography**: Australia, Brisbane
- **Tech maturity**: Reactive
- **Buying motion**: Not a buyer. The persona who has to live with the buyer's choice.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: bepoz or idealpos
- **Solutions they touch**: POS (constantly), payments, loyalty (occasionally), KDS (food orders).

---

## Bio

Jess is in her second year of a nursing degree. She works three nights a week behind the bar to pay rent. She has worked at this pub for 14 months. She has worked at two other hospitality jobs before this. She has never been trained for more than 90 minutes on any POS.

She is fast, she is friendly, she does not care about the technology. She cares about getting through her shift, making her tips, and getting home in time to sleep before a 7am ward shift.

She is the persona that almost every roadmap forgets. She is also the persona that uses the system more than any other persona in the library, by a factor of ten.

---

## A day in the life

A Saturday.

- **17:00**: Arrives, throws her bag in the back. Clocks in on the POS.
- **17:15**: First customer. A round of four schooners. She remembers the buttons. Just.
- **18:30**: Bar fills. She is on one of three terminals. She has a queue of three deep.
- **19:30**: Card payment declines. She has to navigate three screens to retry. The customer is patient. The next two are not.
- **20:30**: Someone wants a loyalty number added to an existing tab. She does not know how. She asks the shift leader. Two minutes lost.
- **22:00**: A regular orders a complex round. She splits it across two payment methods. The split feature works.
- **23:30**: Her station's till floats are short by $40. She and the shift leader recount. It is correct after recount. The system had an open void.
- **00:30**: Closes. Counts tips. Goes home.

---

## Goals

- Get through her shift cleanly.
- Make tips.
- Not get yelled at by a customer because of the system.
- Not get pulled into back-office investigations.
- Keep this job until she finishes her degree.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- The POS has features she has never been taught. She uses 20% of it.
- A declined payment is a stressful, multi-screen recovery while a customer stands and waits.
- Adding a loyalty number to an existing tab is harder than it should be.
- New menu items appear without notice. She has to figure them out at peak.
- Software updates change the screen layout occasionally. She has to relearn the buttons mid-shift.
- The bar manager keeps asking her to use the loyalty prompt. The loyalty prompt slows her down. She does not bother.

---

## KPIs she is judged on

- Implicitly: speed, accuracy, attitude, tip pool contribution.
- Formally: nothing she sees a number for.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in her hand**: A POS terminal, a payment terminal, a glass washer, occasionally a tablet for promos.
- **Current stack**: Whatever the venue runs. She has no input.
- **Comfort level**: Reactive. She will learn what she has to. She will not explore.
- **What she will not tolerate**: Anything that makes a customer wait or makes her look incompetent.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Nothing.
- **Influences**: Whether the system actually gets used. If she works around it, the data is wrong, the loyalty is broken, and the variance is up.
- **Cannot touch**: Everything outside her till.
- **Who she needs on side**: The shift leader and the bar manager.

---

## Quotes

> "If a feature is in the menu I never open, it does not exist."

> "I do not care about loyalty. I care about the queue."

> "When the screen changes overnight and nobody tells me, I look stupid in front of the customer."

---

## What she needs from Oolio

- A till layout where the buttons she actually uses are in the same place every shift.
- A fast, single-screen recovery from a declined payment.
- A loyalty flow that takes less than five seconds and is impossible to forget.
- New menu items pushed with a 60-second screen brief, not a 12-page PDF.
- A system that does not change visually on her without warning.

---

## What loses her

- A version change that moves the buttons.
- A loyalty prompt that costs her ten seconds per transaction.
- A payment flow that needs more than two screens to recover from a decline.
- Being asked to email logs.

---

## Strategic signal

Jess is the persona who exposes the gap between product design and reality. Every Oolio product decision should be tested against the question: does this still work when the person using it has been trained for 90 minutes, works three nights a week, and is two hours into a Saturday rush. If it does not, the feature will be worked around and the data Oolio collects will be wrong. She is the smallest-influence, highest-volume persona in the library. Get her right and almost every metric improves.
