# Josh Bennett

> Runs a concourse bar for four hours flat out.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a concourse bar supervisor at a major Australian stadium to represent the stadia and events frontline. Josh, the venue, and the caterer are invented. The pattern is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Bar Supervisor (casual, event-day), Bay 14 concourse bar, Harbourfield Stadium
- **Age**: 24
- **Location**: Melbourne, Australia
- **Venue**: The same stadium Dani Hartigan runs F&B operations for. His outlet is a twelve-till concourse bar serving one stand.
- **Reports to**: The event-day outlet manager for his stand, and above that Dani's operations team.
- **Manages**: A crew of ten to twelve casuals for the event. The crew changes every event, and every event has someone who has never done it before.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Enterprise (contract-catered stadium; he is a casual on the event-day roster)
- **Service style**: Stadia, events, convention
- **Geography**: Australia, Melbourne
- **Tech maturity**: Functional. Quick on any till after one shift, no interest beyond that.
- **Buying motion**: None. He is a user, never a buyer.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: swiftpos at the outlet, as the stadium's POS. He operates whatever the venue runs.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, tap-to-pay payments (the venue is cashless), mobile order-taking devices for queue-busting, the outlet's stock counts.

---

## Bio

Josh has worked events at the stadium for three seasons, alongside a weekday job in a bottle shop. He started on a till, got fast, kept turning up, and got made a supervisor because the returning casuals who can run a crew are worth their weight in gold. He knows the rhythm of an event night by heart: dead, busy, insane, dead.

His whole world is speed of service. A concourse bar has one job: serve the maximum number of people in the burst windows before the game and at the breaks. Every second added to a transaction, multiplied across twelve tills and twenty minutes of half time, is a visible chunk of queue that does not get served. He does not care what the system is called. He cares that a brand-new casual can be put on a till and be safe and quick within their first ten minutes, and that nothing stops the line moving.

---

## A day in the life

Saturday night football, 19:25 start.

- **14:30**: Clocks in. Finds out which of his crew from last event came back. Four did. Seven are new.
- **15:00**: Outlet check with the outlet manager: tills logged in, payment terminals paired, kegs on, fridges full, tonight's menu and prices showing.
- **15:30**: Ten-minute crew huddle. He teaches the till in the time it takes to pour a beer: tap the product, tap pay, hold the card up, next.
- **17:30**: Gates open. Slow trickle. He uses it to let the new starters do real transactions while there is no pressure.
- **18:40**: Pre-game burst begins. Queues go ten deep across all twelve positions. He floats: fixing mistakes, swapping a printer roll, pulling a struggling new starter off tills and onto restocking.
- **19:25**: The game starts and the concourse empties in minutes. Restock everything. Count what is left of the fast lines.
- **20:05**: Half time. The insane twenty minutes. He opens two mobile tap-to-pay devices and walks the queue taking orders to bust it from the back. Midway through, the mid-strength keg blows and the till is still selling it. He needs it off the menu at all twelve positions, now, not after a phone call.
- **20:30**: Second half. Breathe. Send two crew on breaks. Report the stock situation up the radio.
- **22:00**: Full time trade, then closedown: stock count, terminal shutdown, cleaning.
- **23:30**: Clocks out. Same again Tuesday for the concert.

---

## Goals

- Serve the burst. Every extra transaction in the half-time window is the whole point of his job.
- Get new casuals safe and quick on a till inside ten minutes.
- Never have a customer walk away from his queue because it was not moving.
- Keep being asked back as a supervisor, and keep the good casuals coming back with him.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- Any extra tap on the till. His crew do thousands of transactions a night, and one extra tap is minutes of queue.
- A new crew every event. Whatever training the last crew absorbed walks out the door with them.
- Stock running out mid-event with no fast way to kill the item on every till at once, so his crew keep selling something they cannot pour.
- A payment terminal that hesitates. At his pace, three seconds of spinner feels like an outage.
- Network drops. If the tills stop, twelve queues stop, and he is the one facing them.
- He finds out about menu or price changes for the event at the pre-shift check, sometimes after a customer does.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Not formally measured beyond his casual rating, but felt through:
- Transactions and sales through his outlet in the burst windows, which the operations team watch live.
- Queue length at his bay compared with the bays either side.
- Stock variance at his outlet after the count.
- Whether his crew turn up again, and whether he gets the big events.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: The outlet's POS tills, tap-to-pay payment terminals, a mobile order device for queue-busting, the radio, his own phone.
- **Current stack**: Whatever the stadium runs. He knows it by feel, not by name.
- **Comfort level**: Functional and fast. He learned the till in one shift and can teach it in ten minutes, which is exactly the standard he holds the screen to.
- **What he will not tolerate**: Anything that slows the line. A confusing screen, a slow terminal, a fiddly correction flow.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Nothing. He runs his outlet within the event plan.
- **Influences**: Which casuals get put on tills versus restocking, and, informally, what the operations team hear about how the system behaved at the coalface.
- **Cannot touch**: Menu, prices, system configuration, staffing levels.
- **Who he needs on side**: His outlet manager, the operations team on the radio, and the four returning casuals who anchor his crew.

---

## What they need from Oolio

- The fastest possible transaction flow. Product, pay, done. Nothing in the way.
- A screen a first-timer can operate safely within ten minutes of walking in.
- One-touch, everywhere-at-once item kill when stock runs out mid-event.
- Payment that is instant and keeps working, or fails obviously with a clear next step, when the network wobbles.
- Mobile and tap-to-pay queue-busting that works the same way the fixed tills do, so there is nothing new to learn.

---

## What loses them

- An extra tap in the core sale flow. Any reason for it. It costs queue.
- A till that behaves differently from the one next to it.
- Corrections or refunds that need a manager code for trivial mistakes at peak.
- Silence when something fails. A frozen screen with no explanation, in front of a ten-deep queue.
- Anything that assumes the operator was trained beyond twenty minutes.

---

## Strategic signal

Josh is the floor of usability for the stadia vertical, the same job Kai Williams does for QSR and the frontline bartender does for pubs, at the highest transaction pace in hospitality. Stadia is swiftpos's market, and it is won or lost in Josh's burst windows: the platform either serves the half-time queue or it does not. Every order-entry, payments, and offline decision that touches stadia should be tested against a crew of seven first-timers and a twenty-minute window.
