# Karl Becker

> Five bars under one roof. Throughput is the only metric.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Bar Manager, at a flagship enterprise pub (same group as Liam Henderson and Priya Sharma)
- **Age**: 35
- **Location**: London, United Kingdom
- **Venue**: A 600+ capacity flagship venue with five separate bar areas, a beer garden bar, and a function-room bar.
- **Reports to**: The Deputy GM (Priya Sharma's analogue at this venue) and ultimately the GM.
- **Manages**: A pool of ~25 bar staff across shifts, four bar leads (one per bar area), one cellar specialist.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Enterprise chain, 1 of 620 venues, at a flagship site
- **Service style**: Pub and hotel (multi-revenue)
- **Geography**: United Kingdom, London
- **Tech maturity**: Functional
- **Buying motion**: Procurement-led (he is a user, not a buyer)

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: bepoz, multi-revenue-centre setup. Chosen by head office.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS (multi-terminal, multi-bar), payments, stock and cellar management, loyalty (centrally controlled), labour scheduling.

---

## Bio

Karl moved from Germany to London at 22 and has worked in large UK pub venues ever since. He has been at this flagship for six years, three as bar manager. The venue is the busiest in the group on a per-night basis. On a derby night the bar takes more revenue than 90% of the estate's other venues combined.

He thinks about bar service the way a logistics manager thinks about a warehouse. Flow, queue depth, station design, dwell time at the till. He is not a cocktail artist. He runs an operation.

He has been offered a regional bar standards role twice and turned it down. He likes being in the venue. He has thoughts about being a GM in five years and is taking the development modules quietly.

---

## A day in the life

A Friday with no event.

- **14:00**: Arrives. Reads the daily plan. Reviews staff allocation across the five bars.
- **15:00**: Cellar check with his cellar specialist. Two lines to be cleaned. One keg change due in the gaming bar.
- **16:00**: Pre-shift briefing. Bar leads, station assignments, the night's promotions.
- **17:00**: Doors open for after-work trade. Three bars staffed, two on standby.
- **19:30**: All five bars live. He walks the floor every 15 minutes timing the queues. The main bar is at 80 seconds queue time, target is 60.
- **20:30**: He opens a sixth till on the main bar. Queue drops to 45 seconds.
- **22:30**: A POS terminal in the back bar hangs. The cellar specialist swaps it for a spare. Two minutes lost.
- **00:30**: Bar closes in the main areas. Late bar in the function room continues. He runs the cellar yield report.
- **02:00**: He hands over to the night manager and leaves.

---

## Goals

- Hit the head-office throughput targets per bar, every shift.
- Cellar yield in the top decile of the estate.
- Stock variance within head-office tolerance every week.
- Zero peak-hour incidents during a televised event.
- A bar lead bench strong enough to run a Saturday without him.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- The POS workflow for a round of four pints at peak is one click too long. He has told head office. The new version is in pilot in two other venues. He is not in the pilot.
- Stock variance reporting is granular at venue level and almost useless at bar-within-venue level. He has to build the per-bar view himself.
- Cellar yield reporting is delayed by 24 hours. He wants it live.
- Loyalty at the bar is fiddly. Most regulars do not bother.
- His best feedback to head office takes weeks to reach the team that builds the product.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Throughput per bar per hour at peak.
- Cellar yield per line.
- Stock variance weekly.
- GP% per bar.
- Wage% on the bar.
- Customer feedback on bar service.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: A radio, a corporate phone, the POS at every bar, the cellar management tablet, a back-office PC.
- **Current stack**: Enterprise POS configured for multi-bar, integrated payments, cellar management, head office stock and ordering, head office labour tool, audit app.
- **Comfort level**: Functional. He has run the same platform for years. He knows where it bends.
- **What he will not tolerate**: A queue that grows because of the till. A keg yield drop he cannot diagnose.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Bar-level operational decisions. Bar staff hires within budget. Bar-level supplier requests within agreed framework.
- **Influences**: Venue-level changes to bar workflow. He is the voice on bar matters within the venue.
- **Cannot touch**: POS, payments, supplier list, menu, pricing.
- **Who he needs on side**: The Deputy GM and the GM. His cellar specialist. His bar leads.

---

## Quotes

> "One click costs you ten pints an hour. Ten pints an hour costs you the season."

> "Cellar yield drops do not have ten causes. They have three. Tell me which one tonight."

> "Loyalty at a busy bar is fine in theory. In practice it is a queue."

---

## What he needs from Oolio

- A POS round-throughput workflow tested in venues like his.
- Per-bar reporting at the bar level, not the venue level.
- Live cellar yield, not next-day.
- A loyalty flow that takes less than five seconds at the bar.
- A way for his feedback to actually reach the product team.

---

## What loses him

- A POS workflow change that adds clicks to a round.
- A cellar yield discrepancy he cannot get answers on.
- A loyalty product that interrupts service.
- An incident response that takes more than ten minutes during peak.

---

## Strategic signal

Karl is the persona who proves the platform at the highest-volume bar in the estate. If Oolio's POS can run his Friday, it can run almost any UK pub bar. He is also a credibility validator: enterprise prospects will ask for references like his venue, and his sign-off on the platform is gold. Investment in bar-throughput workflow is investment in his persona.
