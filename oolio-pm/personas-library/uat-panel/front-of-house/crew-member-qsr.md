# Kai Williams

> Seventeen, on the counter and drive-thru. Learns by doing.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a McDonald's crew member to represent the QSR frontline. Kai is an invented individual. The brand is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Crew member (casual, hourly). Front counter, kiosks, and drive-thru
- **Age**: 17
- **Location**: Western Sydney, Australia
- **Venue**: The same flagship McDonald's Steph Nguyen manages, in Ray Tan's franchise group
- **Reports to**: The shift manager on duty, and the restaurant manager.
- **Manages**: Nobody. This is a first job.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Mid-market group, 1 of 9 restaurants, franchised global QSR brand
- **Service style**: QSR
- **Geography**: Australia, Sydney
- **Tech maturity**: Reactive. Whatever is on the screen is what he uses. No interest in the system, only in getting through the shift.
- **Buying motion**: None. He is a user, never a buyer.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits the OOLIO platform front counter, kiosk support, and drive-thru order points. This is a fit assessment for the frontline of a QSR.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS front counter, self-ordering kiosks (helping customers who get stuck), drive-thru order screen and headset, payments.

---

## Bio

Kai is in year twelve and works three or four shifts a week. This is his first job. He was trained on the front counter in a couple of short sessions and most of what he knows he picked up on the floor, watching the older crew and being told quickly when he got something wrong.

He is not nervous about the technology, he just wants it to be obvious. When a customer is standing there and a queue is building behind them, he does not have time to think. He needs the screen to show him what to tap. When something goes wrong, a wrong item, a payment that will not go through, he wants to fix it without having to call a manager over every time, because that feels slow and a bit embarrassing.

---

## A day in the life

A Friday after school.

- **16:00**: Clocks in. Put on the front counter for the after-school rush.
- **16:20**: A customer changes their order halfway through. Kai has to back one item out. He taps carefully so he does not void the whole order.
- **17:00**: Moves to help on the kiosks, showing an older customer how to add a meal and pay.
- **18:00**: Dinner peak. Switched to drive-thru order-taking on the headset and screen. Cars back to back.
- **18:40**: A payment declines at the window. He is not sure what to do and waves the shift manager over.
- **19:30**: Quieter spell. Restocks, wipes down, watches the order screen for delivery and dine-in.
- **20:30**: Last hour. Tired, moving on autopilot, relying entirely on the screen to keep him right.
- **21:00**: Clocks out.

---

## Goals

- Get through a busy shift without holding up the queue.
- Not make mistakes that need a manager to come and fix.
- Learn the till and drive-thru well enough to feel confident.
- Pick up extra shifts, which means being seen as reliable and quick.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- When the screen layout is not obvious, he hesitates, and a queue builds behind a hesitation.
- Fixing a simple mistake, a wrong item or a change of mind, sometimes needs a manager, which feels slow.
- A declined or failed payment leaves him stuck and embarrassed at the window.
- He was trained quickly, so anything that is not on the screen in front of him, he simply does not know.
- At the end of a long shift, anything fiddly is where errors creep in.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Not formally measured as an individual, but felt through:
- Speed at the counter and drive-thru during his shifts.
- Order accuracy, whether his orders come back as remakes.
- Whether shift managers trust him to work peak stations.
- Reliability and attitude, which decide whether he gets more shifts.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: The front-counter POS, the drive-thru screen and headset, the kiosks when helping customers. His own phone in his pocket.
- **Current stack**: Whatever the restaurant runs. He neither knows nor cares what it is called.
- **Comfort level**: Reactive, but a fast learner on anything visual and obvious. He is a digital native, so a clear screen he gets instantly. An unclear one he does not.
- **What he will not tolerate**: Feeling stupid in front of a customer because the screen is confusing.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Nothing.
- **Influences**: Nothing directly. His struggle is the truest test of whether a screen is actually usable.
- **Cannot touch**: Everything is set by the brand, the franchisee, and the manager.
- **Who he needs on side**: The shift manager, who he relies on when something goes wrong.

---

## Quotes

> "Just show me what to press. There is someone waiting right in front of me."

> "I do not want to call the manager over for every little thing. It makes everyone wait."

> "If I have seen it once, I am fine. If it is hidden, I have no idea it is even there."

---

## What they need from Oolio

- A counter and drive-thru screen so obvious that a first job at seventeen is enough to use it.
- Simple, safe ways to fix a normal mistake without a manager every time.
- A payment flow that is clear about what happened and what to do when it fails.
- Nothing that assumes training he never got.

---

## What loses them

- A cluttered or unpredictable screen that makes him hesitate at peak.
- Hiding everyday actions behind menus a quickly-trained casual will never find.
- Error messages that say something failed but not what to do.
- Anything that makes him look slow or foolish in front of a customer.

---

## Strategic signal

Kai is the floor of usability for the entire QSR vertical. The franchisor buys, the franchisee rolls out, the manager runs it, but it is Kai's hands on the till at peak. If a casual on a first job, trained in two short sessions, cannot use a screen cleanly under pressure, the design has failed, no matter how it tested internally. He is the QSR equivalent of the bartender and line-cook personas: the frontline reality every order-entry decision must survive.
