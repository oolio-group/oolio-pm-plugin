# Sofia Marchetti

> Fourteen kitchens, one menu. Fights spec drift for a living.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Kitchen Operations Director, Fratelli Hospitality Group (a 14-venue casual-dining group, Italian-leaning, across South East Queensland)
- **Age**: 39
- **Location**: Brisbane, Australia
- **Venue**: Head office above the flagship restaurant, plus a small central production kitchen in an industrial unit. She is in two or three venues a day, every day.
- **Reports to**: The group's Operations Director, alongside peers running FOH standards, people, and marketing.
- **Manages**: Fourteen venue head chefs (dotted line through their venue managers), the central production kitchen team of six, and a group purchasing coordinator.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Mid-market group, 14 venues, one brand
- **Service style**: Casual dining restaurant
- **Geography**: Australia, Brisbane and South East Queensland
- **Tech maturity**: Deliberate. The group chose its stack and she was in the room. IT is still somebody's part-time job.
- **Buying motion**: Functional committee (Ops, Finance, and her; the founder still signs the big cheques)

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits the OOLIO platform or idealpos for a casual-dining group at this scale.
- **Solutions they touch**: KDS in every kitchen, menu and recipe management, stock and ordering, supplier catalogues, food cost reporting, and the POS insofar as menu changes flow through it.

## Bio

Sofia was a head chef by 28, ran the group's flagship kitchen for five years, and moved to the group role when the founder realised that fourteen kitchens run by fourteen strong-willed chefs had become fourteen slightly different restaurants. Her mandate is consistency: the same dish, at the same spec, at the same cost, in Toowoomba as in Fortitude Valley.

Her enemy is drift. A head chef substitutes a cheaper passata and the dish changes. A supplier short-delivers and a venue improvises. A menu update goes out by email and three venues are still selling the old dish a fortnight later. None of it is malice; all of it is entropy. She built the central production kitchen to take the highest-risk items (sauces, braises, desserts) out of venue hands entirely, and she spends the rest of her time closing the gap between the recipe as written and the plate as served. She is the whole back-of-house function for a group too big for chef-by-chef trust and too small for an enterprise tooling team.

## A day in the life

A Tuesday. Menu change week: the winter menu goes live across all venues on Thursday.

- **06:30**: At the central production kitchen. Checks the overnight run of ragù and the batch labels. Two venues have under-ordered against their forecast.
- **08:00**: Desk time. Reviews last week's food cost by venue. Twelve venues sit between 27% and 29%. One is at 31.4% for the third week. She books herself in there for Thursday.
- **09:30**: Winter menu call with all fourteen head chefs. Walks the new specs, yields, and plating photos. Fields the same three objections she heard at the tasting.
- **11:00**: Drives to the Chermside venue. Line check during prep: portion scales out, spec sheets up, but the old menu's garnish is still being prepped. Corrects it before Thursday, not after.
- **13:00**: Eats the two new dishes at the pass, cooked by the venue's CDP, not the head chef. That is the real test.
- **14:30**: Supplier meeting. The group's mozzarella supplier has raised prices 9%. She has a trial batch from an alternative and a spreadsheet of what the switch does to cost and spec across 23 recipes.
- **16:00**: Builds Thursday's menu cutover checklist: recipes updated centrally, POS and KDS updates confirmed per venue, old items end-dated, allergen matrix reissued.
- **18:00**: Dinner service at the flagship, watching the KDS during the early rush. New dish ticket times look fine.
- **19:30**: Home. Answers two head chef texts about Thursday. There are always two.

## Goals

- The same dish at the same spec in every venue, verifiable without her having to stand in fourteen kitchens.
- Food cost held in the target band in every venue, not just on the group average.
- Menu changes that land in all fourteen venues on the same day, cleanly, with no venue selling ghosts of the old menu.
- Central production carrying more of the risk and the venues carrying less of it.
- A bench of head chefs who run the spec because the system makes it easy, not because she is watching.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- Recipe and spec drift. The recipe lives centrally, but what happens on the bench in venue nine on a Friday is invisible until it shows up in cost or a review.
- Food cost percentage arrives per venue, weekly, after the fact. By the time she sees a 31%, it has been bleeding for days, and finding the cause means a site visit.
- Menu rollouts are a checklist she holds together by hand: recipes, POS, KDS, allergen matrix, print, training. One missed step in one venue and the change did not really happen.
- Supplier switching is punishing. One ingredient change touches dozens of recipes, costs, and allergen declarations, and today that is a spreadsheet exercise.
- Central production and venue ordering do not talk well. Venues under-order and then panic; the production kitchen runs on forecasts she assembles manually.
- Fourteen head chefs means fourteen levels of system discipline. The tooling has to survive the least diligent kitchen, not the best one.

---

## KPIs she is judged on

- Food cost percentage, per venue, against a target band.
- Spec compliance and menu consistency (audited by visit, mystery diner, and review scraping).
- Wastage as a percentage of food cost, per venue and at central production.
- Menu change execution: on time, all venues, no rogue items.
- Allergen and food safety incident count, group-wide.
- Head chef retention.

## Tech profile

- **Devices in her hand**: Laptop, phone, the venue KDS and back-office screen wherever she is standing, portion scales and probes in every kitchen.
- **Current stack**: Group POS with KDS in every venue, a recipe and stock system only half-adopted across venues, supplier ordering portals, spreadsheets that bridge every gap, the group's accounting system for the numbers that matter.
- **Comfort level**: Deliberate. She built the group's recipe database herself and knows exactly where the tooling ends and her spreadsheets begin.
- **What she will not tolerate**: Systems that ask a busy venue kitchen for data entry it will not do. Anything unmaintained rots in a fortnight.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Recipes, specs, suppliers within budget, central production investment within her opex, kitchen equipment to a threshold.
- **Influences**: The group's POS, KDS, and stock platform choices heavily. She is the committee member the founder asks "will the kitchens actually use it".
- **Cannot touch**: Menu pricing (with Finance and the founder), venue capex, FOH systems.
- **Who she needs on side**: The Operations Director, the founder, Finance, and above all the fourteen head chefs, because a rollout they resent is a rollout that quietly fails.

## What she needs from Oolio

- One central recipe and menu source that pushes to POS and KDS in every venue on a date she sets, with proof of what each venue is actually running.
- Food cost and wastage per venue, per week at worst, with enough granularity to find the cause without a site visit.
- Supplier and ingredient changes that cascade through recipes, costs, and allergen data in one move.
- Support for a central production model: batch items produced centrally, ordered by venues, costed properly at both ends.
- Tooling light enough for the least disciplined kitchen in the group, because that kitchen sets the real adoption rate.
- Rollout and training that respects that she is one person covering fourteen sites, not a project team.

---

## What loses her

- A recipe module that assumes someone in each venue will maintain it. Nobody will.
- Reporting at group level only. Averages are where her problems hide.
- A menu change process with manual steps per venue. Fourteen venues means fourteen chances to miss one.
- Allergen data that can drift out of step with the recipe. That is not a bug to her, it is a safety incident waiting.
- A pitch aimed at a single restaurant or at a 600-site chain. She is neither, and she can tell in one meeting.

---

## Strategic signal

Sofia fills the mid-market back-of-house gap flagged in `segments.md`: the tier where a group is too big to run on trust and too small to build its own tooling. This is exactly where a platform earns its keep, because she will trade money for anything that genuinely replaces her spreadsheets, and she evaluates rationally as part of a functional committee. She is also a proving ground for Oolio's expertise ambition: menu consistency, food cost control, and central production are consultancy conversations, not feature lists. Win Sofia and the platform holds the whole middle of the market's kitchen story.
