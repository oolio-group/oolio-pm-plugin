# Marcus Whitehouse

> Runs the pass. Will kill any tool that adds a click.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Head Chef, Lantern Row (the same venue Sam Patel runs as GM)
- **Age**: 41
- **Location**: Sydney, Australia
- **Venue**: Lantern Row. 92-seat modern Australian restaurant.
- **Reports to**: The owner (co-decision-maker on menu and food strategy), and operationally pairs with the GM.
- **Manages**: A kitchen brigade of eight: sous chef, two CDPs, three commis, a kitchen hand, a pastry chef.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Independent, 1 venue
- **Service style**: Fine dining restaurant
- **Geography**: Australia, Sydney
- **Tech maturity**: Functional (cares about the KDS, cares about cost of goods, does not care about anything else)
- **Buying motion**: Has a veto in the buying motion, not a vote

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: ordermate (KDS and at-table ordering matter), with stock and recipe costing.
- **Solutions they touch**: KDS, recipe costing, stock counts, supplier ordering.

---

## Bio

Marcus has been a chef for 22 years. He has worked under two of Australia's most-respected chefs, run a kitchen in London for three years, came home to a head chef role, and has been at Lantern Row for five years. He is the reason the restaurant has the reputation it has.

He is a professional. He is direct. He is not interested in software. He is interested in food, in cost, and in the discipline of the kitchen. If the KDS distracts a CDP for half a second, he will tell you about it.

He has opinions about technology that are precise and uncompromising. Anything that improves the kitchen's clarity, speed, or cost control, he will use. Anything that does not, he will route around or ignore.

---

## A day in the life

A Saturday.

- **08:00**: Arrives. Walks the cool room. Checks deliveries against the order. Two boxes of fish are not what was ordered.
- **09:30**: Calls the supplier. Resolves.
- **10:00**: Morning prep starts. He walks the brigade through the day's specials.
- **12:00**: Lunch service. He is on the pass.
- **14:30**: Lunch close. He sits with the sous chef and reviews the day so far. Goes through what to prep for dinner.
- **16:30**: Costs out a new dish in his head. Asks the sous chef to weigh and measure it. The cost goes into the recipe tool.
- **17:30**: Pre-service. Briefs the brigade.
- **19:30**: Dinner peak. The KDS is on. He calls the orders verbally as well. Two tickets come in with an allergen modifier flagged in the wrong place on the screen. He fixes by voice.
- **22:00**: Service winding down. He walks the floor briefly, talks to two tables.
- **23:30**: Closes the kitchen. Final fridge check. Goes home.

## Goals

- A menu that wins the right kind of attention.
- Food GP at 70% or better.
- A kitchen brigade that develops under him and stays for years.
- A reputation that translates into his own restaurant, eventually.
- A clean service. Every service.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- KDS modifier handling is the single biggest issue. A misplaced modifier on a ticket is an allergen risk and a remake.
- Recipe costing requires manual updates whenever a supplier price changes. He delegates this and it slips.
- Stock counts are a chore done by his sous chef and they take too long.
- The POS rings food in fast but sometimes drops modifiers between the FOH terminal and the KDS.
- Supplier ordering is split across an email inbox, two supplier portals, and a stock tool.
- Nobody in the wider supply chain seems to understand that a 9pm Saturday is not the time to test new things.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Food cost as a percentage of food revenue.
- Wastage.
- Comp and remake counts.
- Allergen incident count (zero is the only acceptable number).
- Staff retention.
- Reputation (informal but the owner tracks it).

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: A KDS screen on the pass, a back-office PC, his phone, a printer (still, in some cases).
- **Current stack**: A KDS integrated to the POS, a recipe costing module, a stock counting tool, supplier portals, WhatsApp with suppliers.
- **Comfort level**: Functional. Will master what matters. Will not engage with anything that does not.
- **What he will not tolerate**: A KDS that drops orders. A modifier that does not arrive as written.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Kitchen-related operational decisions. Supplier choice within agreed list. Kitchen hires up to a defined level.
- **Influences**: KDS and recipe-costing tool choice. The GM and the owner will not change either without him.
- **Cannot touch**: Capital. POS choice (he gets a veto but the GM and owner drive it).
- **Who he needs on side**: The owner. The GM. His sous chef.

## Quotes

---

## What he needs from Oolio

- A KDS that is rock solid on modifiers, allergens, and order routing.
- Recipe costing that updates from supplier price changes automatically, or close to it.
- Stock counts the sous chef can do in 30 minutes, not 90.
- A supplier ordering experience that consolidates the mess.
- A platform that respects that Saturday at 9pm is not the time to push updates.

---

## What loses him

- A KDS update that loses a single order.
- A modifier handling bug.
- A recipe costing module that disagrees with itself.
- An update pushed during service.

---

## Strategic signal

Marcus is the persona that holds the line on operational reliability. The COO buys reliability, the GM verifies it, Marcus is the persona that proves it. If Oolio invests in the KDS as a serious product, Marcus is the persona who validates the investment. If Oolio under-invests in BOH, the food side of the business will quietly route around the platform, the data will be wrong, and the consultancy proposition collapses, because no consultancy that does not understand cost of goods is taken seriously by people like Marcus.
