# Lucy Tran

> Six venues, one accounting file, zero tolerance for variance.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Finance Manager, Harbour Lane Group
- **Age**: 45
- **Location**: Melbourne, Australia
- **Venue**: Harbour Lane Group. Six venues: two cafes, a pub, two casual dining restaurants, and a rooftop bar. Head office is two desks in the back of the pub.
- **Reports to**: The founder and Managing Director.
- **Manages**: A part-time accounts payable assistant, plus the relationship with the group's external accountant and payroll provider.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Small group, 6 venues
- **Service style**: Mixed. Cafes, pub, casual dining, bar.
- **Geography**: Australia, Melbourne
- **Tech maturity**: Deliberate. She chose the accounting stack herself and defends it.
- **Buying motion**: Founder-and-ops. The founder signs, but nothing financial gets bought or replaced without her.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Mixed POS across the six venues, typical of a small group assembled by acquisition and opening. She does not run the tills. She reconciles what comes out of them, which makes her the group's loudest voice for one platform with one clean export.
- **Solutions they touch**: Payments (settlement and payout reconciliation), POS reporting and exports, accounting integrations, gift cards and loyalty (as liabilities on her balance sheet), delivery aggregator statements.

## Bio

Lucy is a bookkeeper by trade and a registered BAS agent. She spent fifteen years doing the books for Melbourne hospitality businesses from a home office before Harbour Lane, then a two-venue operation, became her biggest client. When the group hit four venues the founder asked her to come in-house. She was the first head office hire. She now closes the month for six venues, runs payroll handover, lodges the BAS, and knows the group's real margin better than anyone, including the founder.

She has never worked a service and does not need to. Her floor is the back office: the accounting file, the bank feeds, the payout reports, the aggregator statements, and a set of spreadsheets she resents needing. Every dollar that moves through a till, a card terminal, a tip jar, or a delivery app eventually lands on her desk, and her job is to make all of it agree. When it does not, she finds out why. Sometimes the answer is a system. Sometimes the answer is a person, and those are the weeks she earns her salary.

---

## A day in the life

The third working day of the month. Close week.

- **07:30**: At her desk before the pub opens. Bank feeds first. The payments payout that landed overnight bundles Friday, Saturday, and Sunday into one line. She starts unpicking it against the POS settlement reports, venue by venue.
- **09:00**: Two venues match to the cent. One is out by $412.80. She pulls the day's refunds and finds it: a duplicate refund, same card, same amount, eleven minutes apart, processed by the same staff member. She notes the name. Third time this quarter.
- **10:30**: Aggregator reconciliation. The delivery platform's statement disagrees with the POS by more than commission again. Adjustments, chargebacks, and a "marketing contribution" nobody agreed to. She raises her fourth dispute this year.
- **12:00**: Tips. Card tips sit in a clearing account until payroll distributes them. One venue's tip clearing balance is growing instead of clearing, which means someone is not running the distribution. She calls the venue manager.
- **14:00**: GST check before BAS. One venue's new lunch menu items were keyed with the wrong tax setting for a fortnight. Small money, but wrong is wrong, and it is her name on the lodgement.
- **15:30**: Month-end journals. Gift card liability by venue. The rooftop bar sold $9,000 of gift cards in December that the POS reporting cannot age, so her spreadsheet does it instead.
- **17:00**: Sends the founder the flash P&L by venue with three comments: one venue's COGS is drifting, the refund pattern needs a conversation, and the aggregator dispute is still open.
- **17:30**: Home. The close is on track for day five. She would like it to be day three.

---

## Goals

- Close the month by day five, then by day three, without adding headcount.
- One version of the truth: POS, payments, bank, and the accounting file agreeing without a spreadsheet in between.
- Payout reconciliation that takes minutes per venue, not hours, with fees, refunds, and chargebacks itemised rather than netted into mystery.
- Catch leakage early: refund abuse, till variances, voids, and tip pools that do not clear are all findable in the data if the data is trustworthy.
- Keep every lodgement clean. Her BAS agent registration is her professional reputation.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- Payouts that bundle multiple trading days into one bank line, with fees netted off invisibly, so she rebuilds gross-to-net by hand for every venue.
- Six venues, more than one POS, and no two exports in the same shape. Her chart of accounts mapping lives in her head and a fragile spreadsheet.
- Card tips that flow into revenue at the terminal and have to be manually reversed out into a clearing account, every day, per venue.
- Aggregator statements that never match the POS: commission, adjustments, refunds funded by whom exactly, all in a PDF designed to be unreconcilable.
- Accounting integrations that sync a daily lump sum and call it done. A lump sum is not a reconciliation, it is a burial.
- Refunds, voids, and discounts that need her to cross-reference three reports to see who did what. The data exists. The view does not.
- Export formats that change without notice after an update, silently breaking the mapping she depends on.

---

## KPIs they are judged on

- Days to close the month, per venue and group.
- Unreconciled items at close, in count and dollars.
- Cash and card variance rates by venue.
- BAS and payroll lodgement accuracy and timeliness. Errors here are not KPI misses, they are professional failures.
- Aggregator and payment disputes raised and recovered.
- The founder's informal one: does he trust the numbers enough to make decisions on them without asking her to double-check.

## Tech profile

- **Devices in their hand**: A laptop with two screens, a calculator she still trusts more than any app, and her phone for chasing venue managers.
- **Current stack**: Xero for the group's accounting, a payroll platform, bank feeds, POS back office reporting from more than one system, payment provider portals, aggregator partner portals, and the spreadsheet layer that glues it all together.
- **Comfort level**: Deliberate. She evaluates software by whether its numbers tie out, and she will find out within a week whether they do.
- **What she will not tolerate**: Numbers that are wrong. Slow she can work with. Wrong, even once, and the system is demoted to something she double-checks forever.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Accounting, bookkeeping, and finance tooling within her budget. Process changes to how venues handle cash, refunds, and tips.
- **Influences**: Any POS, payments, or platform decision. The founder will not sign anything she says will make month-end worse, and her verdict on the accounting integration is treated as fact.
- **Cannot touch**: Venue operations, menus, pricing, and the front-of-house experience. She sees their consequences, not their causes.
- **Who she needs on side**: The founder (signature), the venue managers (whose discipline her numbers depend on), and the external accountant (who blesses the year-end).

## What they need from Oolio

- An accounting integration that posts at the right grain: daily takings by venue, by tender, by tax rate, with fees, tips, refunds, and gift card movements broken out, mapped to her chart of accounts.
- Payout reconciliation built for a human: each bank deposit traceable to its trading days, venues, and fee lines in one view.
- Tips handled as tips end to end: collected at the terminal, held as a liability, reported per staff member, cleared through payroll, never silently mixed into revenue.
- Gift card and loyalty liability reporting she can hand to the external accountant without translation.
- Variance and exception surfacing: refunds, voids, discounts, and till differences by venue and by staff member, so the fraud conversation starts from evidence.
- Exports that are stable, documented, and versioned, because her mappings are load-bearing.

---

## What loses them

- A daily lump-sum sync marketed as an "accounting integration".
- Fees netted out of deposits with no itemisation.
- A tax setting that is hard to get right per item, or easy to get wrong silently.
- Any discrepancy between two reports in the same system. If the platform disagrees with itself, everything it says is now suspect.
- Support that cannot answer a reconciliation question and routes her to a how-to article about the till.

---

## Strategic signal

Lucy is the persona who quietly decides whether a small group stays. Owners buy on the front of house, but groups churn on the back office: if month-end gets harder, the finance manager builds the case to leave and the founder signs it. She is also the strongest internal advocate for consolidation onto one platform, because every extra system is another export she has to map. As Oolio moves from selling tills to being the operating platform for a group, per `_framework/oolio-context.md`, the quality of payments reconciliation and accounting integration is the proof, and Lucy is its judge. Test every payments, reporting, and integration decision against her month-end, not just against service.
