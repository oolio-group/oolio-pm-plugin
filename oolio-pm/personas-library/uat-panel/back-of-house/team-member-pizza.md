# Deng Akok

> Nineteen, on the makeline. Box it, cut it, keep it moving.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a Domino's in-store team member to represent the takeaway and pizza frontline. Deng is an invented individual. The brand is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Team member (casual, hourly). Makeline, oven, cut bench, and counter
- **Age**: 19
- **Location**: Burleigh Heads, Gold Coast, Australia
- **Venue**: The same Domino's store Mia Roberts manages, in Nick Castellano's franchise group
- **Reports to**: The shift runner and the store manager.
- **Manages**: Nobody.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Small group, 1 of 3 stores, franchised pizza brand
- **Service style**: Takeaway and pizza
- **Geography**: Australia, Gold Coast
- **Tech maturity**: Reactive to functional. He follows the makeline screen and the counter prompts, and gets faster the more shifts he works.
- **Buying motion**: None. He is a user, never a buyer.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits the deliverit and OOLIO platform makeline and counter, the KDS and order screens a pizza team member works to. This is a fit assessment for the in-store frontline.
- **Solutions they touch**: KDS and makeline screen, oven timing, cut bench order display, counter POS for pickups, payments.

## Bio

Deng is at TAFE part-time and works at the pizza store four nights a week. He started a few months ago and learned the makeline by standing next to it: read the order off the screen, lay the base, top it to the picture, send it through the oven, cut and box it to match the docket. When it is busy he is also pulled to the counter to hand pickups over and take payment.

He does not think about the system. He thinks about keeping pace with the screen. On a quiet night it is easy. On a Friday the orders stack up and the makeline screen is the only thing keeping him straight on what goes on each pizza and which order it belongs to. When the screen is clear, he is fast. When it is cluttered or an order changes halfway, he slows down, and on the makeline slowing down means pizzas backing up behind him.

---

## A day in the life

A Friday night on the makeline.

- **17:00**: Clocks in. Checks his station, the makeline screen, and the oven are ready.
- **17:30**: Orders building. He reads each ticket off the screen and tops to match.
- **18:30**: Peak. Screen full of orders. He works steadily, base, top, oven, cut, box, next.
- **19:00**: An order is modified after it appears. He has to spot the change on the screen so he does not box the wrong pizza.
- **19:45**: Pulled to the counter to clear a run of pickups, taking payment as he hands them over, then straight back to the makeline.
- **20:30**: An aggregator order and a phone order land close together. He works them in docket order to keep dispatch moving.
- **22:00**: Late orders. Tired, leaning entirely on the screen to keep each pizza right.
- **23:00**: Clean-down and clock out.

---

## Goals

- Keep pace with the makeline screen through peak without falling behind.
- Get every pizza right so nothing comes back as a remake.
- Move cleanly between makeline and counter when he is pulled across.
- Learn the stations well enough to be quick and to pick up more shifts.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- A cluttered or slow makeline screen at peak makes him hesitate, and pizzas stack up behind him.
- An order that changes after it appears is easy to miss, and a missed change is a remake.
- Switching to the counter and taking payment mid-make breaks his rhythm if the screen is not obvious.
- He was trained quickly, so anything not shown plainly on the screen, he does not know.
- Late in the shift, tired, is where mistakes creep in if the screen is not clear.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Not measured individually, but felt through:
- Makeline speed during his shifts.
- Order accuracy, whether his pizzas come back as remakes.
- Whether the shift runner trusts him on the makeline at peak.
- Reliability, which decides whether he gets more shifts.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: The makeline and cut-bench screens, the oven timing, the counter POS when pulled across. His own phone in his pocket.
- **Current stack**: Whatever the store runs. He does not know or care what it is called.
- **Comfort level**: Reactive but improving. A clear screen he reads instantly, being a digital native. A cluttered one slows him.
- **What he will not tolerate**: A screen that makes him guess what goes on a pizza when ten orders are waiting.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Nothing.
- **Influences**: Nothing directly. His pace on the makeline is the truest test of whether the kitchen screen actually works.
- **Cannot touch**: Everything is set by the brand, the franchisee, and the manager.
- **Who he needs on side**: The shift runner, who he turns to when something goes wrong.

## Quotes

> "Ten tickets up and someone wants a pickup. I just need both screens to be obvious."

---

## What they need from Oolio

- A makeline screen so clear a quickly-trained casual can keep pace at peak.
- Order changes that are impossible to miss once a ticket is already up.
- A clean handover between makeline and counter when he is pulled across.
- Nothing that assumes training he never had.

---

## What loses them

- A cluttered or laggy kitchen screen at peak.
- Modifications that do not stand out on an order already on the line.
- Hiding everyday actions where a new casual will never find them.
- A counter flow that breaks his makeline rhythm.

---

## Strategic signal

Deng is the floor of usability for the takeaway and pizza kitchen, the makeline equivalent of the QSR crew member and the line cook. The brand buys, the franchisee rolls out, the manager runs it, but it is Deng's pace on the makeline that turns into delivery time. If a casual a few months in cannot keep up with the kitchen screen at peak, the design has failed whatever the demo showed. Every KDS and makeline decision in this vertical must survive him.
