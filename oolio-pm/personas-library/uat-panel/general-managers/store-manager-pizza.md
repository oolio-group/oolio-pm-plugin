# Mia Roberts

> Runs one Domino's. Makeline in, drivers out, clock always running.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a Domino's store manager to represent the takeaway and pizza vertical. Mia is an invented individual. The brand is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Store Manager, a single Domino's store, one of three in Nick Castellano's franchise group
- **Age**: 27
- **Location**: Burleigh Heads, Gold Coast, Australia
- **Venue**: A busy delivery-and-pickup Domino's. No dine-in. Heavy evening, weekend, and late-night trade.
- **Reports to**: The franchisee and his group (Nick Castellano).
- **Manages**: Two to three shift runners, around 25 team members, and a roster of delivery drivers.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Small group, 1 of 3 stores, under a franchised pizza brand
- **Service style**: Takeaway and pizza
- **Geography**: Australia, Gold Coast
- **Tech maturity**: Functional, edging into deliberate. She runs the store on the screens and knows where they slow her down.
- **Buying motion**: Franchisor-mandated. She runs the systems the brand and franchisee provide. She does not buy.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits deliverit (online ordering, dispatch, kitchen flow) with the OOLIO platform for POS and KDS. Her systems are brand-mandated, so this is a fit assessment.
- **Solutions they touch**: Online ordering, POS, KDS and makeline screens, delivery dispatch and tracking, aggregator tablets, payments, labour and scheduling.

## Bio

Mia worked her way up from team member to shift runner to store manager in five years. She is responsible for one store, and on a busy night that store is a machine with a lot of moving parts: orders landing from online, phone, and aggregators, a makeline that has to keep pace, an oven on a fixed cook time, and drivers who need to leave the moment the box is sealed.

Her whole job is flow. She watches the makeline screen and the dispatch board the way Steph at the QSR watches her drive-thru timers. A backup at the cut bench or a driver stuck at the door ripples straight into delivery time, and delivery time is the number everyone above her cares about. She manages people as hard as she manages the clock, because a short or untrained crew on a Friday is how a night falls apart.

---

## A day in the life

A Saturday night.

- **15:00**: Opens the evening operation. Checks the makeline screens, the dispatch board, the oven, and the aggregator tablets are all live.
- **16:00**: Sets the night's stations and briefs the shift runners. Confirms the driver roster.
- **17:30**: Early orders build. She works the cut bench, keeping make and box moving.
- **18:30**: A team member calls in. She adjusts the roster on the labour tool and re-cuts stations.
- **19:30**: Peak. Online and aggregator orders pouring in. She runs the makeline by eye and the dispatch board by the clock.
- **20:30**: Two drivers back at once, three orders waiting. She sequences runs to protect delivery times.
- **21:30**: An aggregator order and a phone order clash on the same timing. She sorts the kitchen queue so neither slips.
- **23:00**: Late trade easing. Reviews the night's delivery times and labour before close.

---

## Goals

- Keep delivery times down all night by protecting makeline and dispatch flow.
- Hit order accuracy so remakes do not steal makeline capacity at peak.
- Get new team members productive on the makeline and counter quickly.
- Run labour to forecast without leaving the make or the road short.
- Get through a full weekend with no screen, oven, or dispatch failure she cannot recover fast.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- A slow or cluttered makeline screen at peak costs her seconds per pizza, and seconds become minutes on delivery time.
- Orders from online, phone, and aggregators in different places make the kitchen queue harder to run as one.
- Drivers and team members are casual and turn over, so she is always training during service.
- When something fails mid-peak, she has no fast way to see why or get it back.
- The numbers she answers for sit across a few systems, so reviewing a night takes assembling.
- Late-night is where accuracy and labour both slip.

---

## KPIs she is judged on

- Average delivery time for her store.
- Order accuracy and remake rate.
- Labour cost as a percentage of sales.
- Makeline speed and dispatch times at peak.
- Customer ratings and complaints.
- Driver availability through the shift.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in her hand**: The manager terminal, the makeline and dispatch screens, her phone, and whatever station she jumps on.
- **Current stack**: Brand online ordering, POS, KDS and makeline, dispatch and GPS tracking, aggregator tablets, payments, labour and scheduling.
- **Comfort level**: Functional and quick. She learns by running it and teaches her crew the same way.
- **What she will not tolerate**: A makeline or dispatch screen that slows at peak. A system that needs a manual to teach a new casual on a Saturday.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Nothing system-wise. Operationally, she runs the store within the franchisee's rules.
- **Influences**: The franchisee, through her store's delivery times, labour, and what she reports as breaking.
- **Cannot touch**: The platform, the menu, pricing, brand marketing.
- **Who she needs on side**: Her shift runners, her drivers, and the franchisee.

## Quotes

---

## What they need from Oolio

- Makeline and dispatch screens that stay fast and clear at peak.
- One kitchen queue that brings online, phone, and aggregator orders together.
- A makeline and counter a brand-new casual can pick up with almost no training.
- A quick way to see why something failed and recover it without losing the night.
- One simple view of how the shift went.

---

## What loses them

- Any lag or clutter on the makeline or dispatch at peak.
- Orders scattered across separate screens and tablets.
- A system that needs real training to use under pressure.
- A failure with no clear path back to trading.

---

## Strategic signal

Mia is where a pizza and takeaway product is proven on the floor. The brand buys it and the franchisee rolls it out, but it succeeds or fails on her makeline and dispatch board at peak. She is the operational reality check for every online ordering, KDS, and dispatch decision in this vertical, and she anchors the single-store manager view that the franchisee depends on. A feature that cannot hold her Saturday night is not ready.
