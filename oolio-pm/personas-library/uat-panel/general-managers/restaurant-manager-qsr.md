# Stephanie "Steph" Nguyen

> Runs a McDonald's at peak. Seconds and crew are everything.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on a McDonald's restaurant manager to represent the QSR vertical. Steph is an invented individual. The brand is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Restaurant Manager (Business Manager grade), at a flagship McDonald's (drive-thru and McCafé), one of nine in Ray Tan's franchise group
- **Age**: 31
- **Location**: Western Sydney, Australia
- **Venue**: A high-volume McDonald's with drive-thru, dine-in, kiosks, McCafé, and delivery. Open long hours, seven days.
- **Reports to**: The franchisee and his operations manager (Ray Tan's group).
- **Manages**: Three to four shift managers and around 80 crew across all dayparts.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Mid-market group, 1 of 9 restaurants, under a franchised global QSR brand
- **Service style**: QSR
- **Geography**: Australia, Sydney
- **Tech maturity**: Functional, edging into deliberate. She knows the systems cold and works around their limits.
- **Buying motion**: Franchisor-mandated. She does not buy. She runs what the brand and the franchisee provide.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits the OOLIO platform (kiosks, KDS, drive-thru, payments) with deliverit for delivery flow. In reality her systems are brand-mandated, so this is a fit assessment.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, self-ordering kiosks, KDS, drive-thru display and timing, payments, labour and scheduling, cash management, delivery tablets.

---

## Bio

Steph started on the front counter at sixteen and never left the brand. She has run every station, every daypart, and every kind of bad night. She is now responsible for one of the busiest restaurants in Ray's group, and she is good at it because she manages two things relentlessly: the clock and the people.

She does not think about technology as technology. She thinks about whether the kiosk is taking orders, whether the KDS is bumping clean, whether the drive-thru timer is green, and whether her newest crew member can work the till without a queue forming. When a system is slow, it is not an IT problem to her, it is a wage and a customer walking out.

---

## A day in the life

A Saturday, the hardest shift of the week.

- **06:45**: Opens. Checks every kiosk, every screen, the drive-thru timers, and the delivery tablets are awake before the first car.
- **08:00**: Breakfast peak. One kiosk freezes. She reboots it between orders and reroutes customers to the counter.
- **10:30**: Changeover to the main menu. Briefs the shift managers on the day's targets and who is on which station.
- **12:00**: Lunch peak. Drive-thru, front counter, kiosks, and delivery all firing at once. She runs the floor by eye and by the timing screens.
- **14:00**: A crew member calls in sick. She re-cuts the roster on the labour tool and pulls someone in.
- **16:00**: Reconciles the morning's cash and reviews the speed-of-service numbers. Two periods missed target. She knows exactly why.
- **18:30**: Dinner build. Delivery orders surge. She watches the KDS queue depth like a hawk.
- **21:30**: Close-down checks. Logs the kiosk that failed for the maintenance report.

---

## Goals

- Hit speed-of-service targets at the drive-thru and kiosks through every peak.
- Keep order accuracy high so remakes and refunds stay low.
- Get her newest crew productive on the till and the makeline as fast as possible.
- Run labour to forecast without leaving the floor short at peak.
- Get through a full weekend with no system falling over.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- A frozen kiosk or a lagging KDS at peak turns directly into queues, walkouts, and wage cost.
- Training a new casual on the systems takes time she does not have during service.
- When a screen or terminal fails, she has no quick way to see why or to get it back fast.
- Delivery orders arriving on separate tablets pull her crew's attention away from the counter and drive-thru.
- The numbers she is judged on live in several systems, so reviewing a shift takes longer than it should.
- Cash management at close is fiddly and error-prone after a long day.

---

## KPIs she is judged on

- Speed of service, drive-thru and kiosk, in seconds.
- Order accuracy and remake or refund rate.
- Labour cost as a percentage of sales for her restaurant.
- Crew retention and time-to-productive for new starters.
- Customer satisfaction and complaint scores.
- Cash and stock variance.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in her hand**: The manager terminal, a headset, her phone, and whatever station she jumps on.
- **Current stack**: Brand POS, kiosks, KDS, drive-thru timing, payments, labour and scheduling, cash management, delivery aggregator tablets.
- **Comfort level**: Functional and fast. She does not read manuals, she learns by doing and teaches the same way.
- **What she will not tolerate**: A system that is slow at peak, or that needs a manual to teach a sixteen-year-old on a Saturday.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Nothing in terms of systems. Operationally, she runs the restaurant day to day within the franchisee's rules.
- **Influences**: The franchisee and operations manager, through how the restaurant performs and what she tells them is breaking.
- **Cannot touch**: The platform, the menu, pricing, or anything brand-level.
- **Who she needs on side**: Her shift managers, her crew, and the franchisee's operations manager.

---

## Quotes

> "If the kiosk is slow, I am paying staff to watch a queue build. That is the whole problem in one sentence."

> "I have ninety minutes to make a new kid useful. The till has to teach itself."

> "Green timers and a full crew. That is a good day."

---

## What they need from Oolio

- Systems that stay fast at the exact moment service peaks.
- A till and makeline a brand-new casual can use with almost no training.
- A clear, quick way to see why something failed and get it back trading.
- Delivery orders that flow into the same kitchen view, not a row of separate tablets.
- One simple view of how the shift actually went.

---

## What loses them

- Any lag, freeze, or extra tap during peak service.
- A system that needs real training to use.
- An error screen that does not tell her what to do next.
- A change that lands mid-shift with no warning.

---

## Strategic signal

Steph is where a QSR product proves itself or fails. The brand may buy it and the franchisee may roll it out, but it lives or dies on her floor at peak. She is the operational reality check for every kiosk, KDS, drive-thru, and delivery decision in the QSR vertical. A feature that cannot survive her Saturday is not ready, whatever it looked like in the demo.
