# Danielle "Dani" Hartigan

> Months of config, then 50,000 people in four hours.

> Benchmark archetype. Modelled on the contract-catered food and beverage operation of a major Australian stadium to represent the stadia and events vertical. Dani, the venue, and the caterer are invented. The pattern is the operating context, not an Oolio customer claim.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: Venue Operations Manager, Food and Beverage, Harbourfield Stadium (a 53,000-seat multi-purpose stadium; F&B operated under contract by Meridian Catering Group)
- **Age**: 41
- **Location**: Melbourne, Australia
- **Venue**: A city stadium hosting 45+ major events a year (football codes, cricket, concerts) plus functions. Around 240 points of sale across concourse outlets, bars, kiosks, and mobile carts.
- **Reports to**: The Catering Director who owns the stadium contract for Meridian.
- **Manages**: A permanent team of eight (outlet managers, a staffing coordinator, a POS and systems administrator), and on event days a casual workforce that can reach 900.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Enterprise (national contract caterer; the stadium is one flagship contract among many)
- **Service style**: Stadia, events, convention
- **Geography**: Australia, Melbourne
- **Tech maturity**: Deliberate. She has configured, broken, and rebuilt event setups. She has opinions and a test plan.
- **Buying motion**: Procurement-led (caterer head office decides, with the venue client's sign-off; she is the loudest voice on operational fit)

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: swiftpos. High-throughput, multi-outlet stadia and events is swiftpos's home ground.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS across every outlet, payments (the venue trades cashless), self-ordering kiosks, stock and ordering, member and loyalty pricing tiers, event reporting.

## Bio

Dani started pulling beers at a suburban footy club at 18, ran bars and functions through her twenties, moved into stadium catering as an outlet manager at 29, and has run F&B operations at Harbourfield for six years. She has worked grand finals, sold-out concerts, and a summer of cricket. Nothing about a big crowd frightens her. Systems failing during one does.

Her job has a rhythm unlike any restaurant. For days at a time the stadium is empty and her work is configuration: building the menu, price tiers, and outlet maps for the next event, checking every terminal, planning the staffing waves. Then an event day arrives and the venue does a month of normal-venue trade in about four hours, through hundreds of POS points, operated largely by casuals who got twenty minutes of training. Her success is measured in whether those four hours run clean. There is no second sitting. Revenue not taken at half time is gone forever.

## A day in the life

An event day. Saturday night football, expected crowd 49,000, 19:25 start.

- **07:00**: Arrives. Walks the concourses. Checks overnight deliveries landed at the right outlets.
- **08:30**: Sits with her systems administrator and verifies the event configuration: tonight's menu is live at every outlet, member pricing tiers are correct, the two closed bays are disabled, the per-event price changes from the caterer's commercial team are in.
- **10:00**: Test transactions at a sample of terminals in every stand. One outlet is showing last event's menu. They fix it and re-check the whole bay.
- **13:00**: First casual staffing wave arrives. Briefings run in groups of forty. Many have never worked this venue. Some have never worked a till.
- **15:30**: Supervisors, including the concourse bar leads, do their outlet checks: floats are not needed (cashless), but log-ins, printers, and payment terminals are.
- **17:30**: Gates open. Trade starts as a trickle, builds fast.
- **19:10**: Pre-game peak. Every queue is ten deep. A bank of six terminals in bay 23 loses network. The outlet keeps trading offline while her systems admin works the problem. She is on the radio the whole time.
- **20:05**: Half time. The burst. The whole night's result is decided in these twenty minutes.
- **22:00**: Full time. Outlets close in sequence. Stock counts start.
- **23:30**: Preliminary sales flash by outlet. She already knows which stands underperformed and wants to know why before the caterer's morning report.
- **01:00**: Locks off. Home. The next event is in five days, and its configuration starts Monday.

## Goals

- Run event days where the system is never the story. Crowds, queues, and weather she can manage. Outages she cannot.
- Get spend per head up event on event, mostly by killing queue time.
- Make every event configuration right the first time, so event day is execution, not firefighting.
- Build a casual workforce that comes back, because a returning casual is twice as fast as a new one.
- Hand her Catering Director a clean, believable per-event result the morning after.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- Per-event configuration is heavy. Menus, prices, member tiers, and outlet maps change event to event, and one missed outlet means wrong prices in front of 49,000 people.
- A mid-event failure is catastrophic, not inconvenient. Ten minutes of downtime at half time is unrecoverable revenue and a stadium full of complaints.
- Her operators got twenty minutes of training. Any screen that needs more than that fails on the night.
- Member and loyalty pricing tiers multiply everything: every product effectively has several prices, and errors surface as angry members at a till.
- Stock visibility mid-event is poor. She finds out an outlet ran out of a line when the queue does.
- Reconciliation across 240 points of sale, hundreds of casual operators, and multiple price tiers takes her team most of the next morning.

---

## KPIs she is judged on

- Spend per head, per event, against the caterer's target.
- Transaction speed and queue performance at peak (increasingly measured, not just felt).
- System uptime during trading windows. Effectively zero tolerance.
- Labour cost per event against the staffing plan.
- Stock variance per event.
- Clean, on-time per-event reporting to the Catering Director and the venue client.

---

## Tech profile

- **Devices in her hand**: A radio, her phone, a tablet with live sales by outlet, and any of 240 POS terminals when she is helping clear a queue.
- **Current stack**: Enterprise venue POS across all outlets, integrated cashless payments, self-ordering kiosks in some stands, a stock system, a casual workforce scheduling platform, the caterer's reporting layer.
- **Comfort level**: Deliberate. She can build an event configuration herself and often does, because she trusts her own checking.
- **What she will not tolerate**: Anything that can take an outlet down mid-event, and any configuration workflow where an error can hide until gates open.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Event-day operational decisions, casual hiring, outlet layouts, the event configuration itself.
- **Influences**: The caterer's POS and payments choices heavily. Head office will not sign a platform she says cannot survive a half time.
- **Cannot touch**: The POS contract, payment rates, sponsor pouring arrangements, menu pricing strategy. Those sit with the Catering Director and head office.
- **Who she needs on side**: Her Catering Director, her systems administrator, her outlet managers and event-day supervisors, and the venue's own operations team.

## What she needs from Oolio

- Proven burst-load reliability: hundreds of terminals at full pace simultaneously, with real evidence, not a demo.
- Offline resilience at the outlet. If the network drops, the till keeps selling and reconciles later.
- Event configuration that is fast, cloneable from a previous event, and checkable before gates open, including member price tiers.
- A till flow a twenty-minute-trained casual can operate at speed.
- Live mid-event visibility: sales and stock by outlet, so she can move staff and product before queues tell her.
- Reconciliation that handles hundreds of operators and multiple price tiers without a morning of spreadsheets.

---

## What loses her

- Any mid-event outage story, anywhere, without a convincing account of what changed.
- Configuration that must be rebuilt from scratch per event, or that cannot be verified before doors.
- A till flow with even one unnecessary tap. Multiply it by 60,000 transactions.
- Updates pushed anywhere near an event day.
- A vendor who has clearly never stood on a concourse at half time.

---

## Strategic signal

Dani is the operational heart of the stadia and events vertical, the market swiftpos exists to serve. Stadia is the most extreme test of the platform anywhere in the library: burst load, casual operators, cashless payments, per-event configuration, and zero tolerance for downtime. If a feature survives Dani's half time, it survives anything. She is also the gatekeeper for the vertical: contract caterers run many venues, and a platform that wins her confidence at one stadium is positioned for the caterer's whole portfolio.
