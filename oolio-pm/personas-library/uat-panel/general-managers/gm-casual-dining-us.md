# Danielle "Dee" Alvarez

> Runs a thousand covers a night, in dollars, plus tax.

> Benchmark archetype. The library's first United States persona, written to test whether assumptions shaped in Australia and the UK travel. Dee, her venue, and her employer are invented; no US customer claim is made. Her world is American, and the prose stays in the library's British English house style by design, so expect US pricing, tipping, and compliance described in Australian spelling.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: General Manager, Cedar and Smoke
- **Age**: 38
- **Location**: Austin, Texas, United States
- **Venue**: Cedar and Smoke. A 280-seat smokehouse and casual dining restaurant with a 90-seat patio and a full bar, just off South Congress. Lunch and dinner, seven days. Average check US$34. Roughly US$9 million a year.
- **Reports to**: The two owners, a chef-founder and an investor partner, who are in the building maybe twice a week.
- **Manages**: Three assistant managers, a bar manager, a kitchen manager, and around 85 staff across servers, bartenders, hosts, bussers, and kitchen. Most are tipped employees.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Independent, 1 venue (high volume)
- **Service style**: Casual dining restaurant
- **Geography**: United States, Austin, Texas
- **Tech maturity**: Functional, trending Deliberate. She uses what works and replaces what does not, but has no time for projects.
- **Buying motion**: Owner-led, with strong GM influence. The owners will pick what Dee picks, because Dee is the one who has to run it.

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: Profile suits the OOLIO platform (all-in-one POS and payments for casual dining) with Oolio Pay. The United States is a named Oolio geography with US support coverage, so this is a target-market fit assessment, not an installed-base story.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, payments, KDS, online ordering, delivery aggregator integrations, waitlist and reservations, tips and labour reporting.

## Bio

Dee started hosting at a Tex-Mex chain at 17, served through college, and never left the industry. She managed two casual dining restaurants in Houston before taking the GM job at Cedar and Smoke four years ago. She has done every job in the building except pitmaster, and the pitmaster knows she would have a go if asked.

Her restaurant is a volume machine. A Saturday runs over a thousand covers, the patio fills even on a 101-degree evening, and the bar prints money during college football season. Almost every transaction is a card, almost every card carries a tip, and every price on the menu is plus tax. Her staff live on tips, which makes the tip pool the single most sensitive system in the building: get it wrong on payday and her best servers are working somewhere else by Friday. She thinks in covers, table turns, labour percentage, and whether the ice machine can keep up. It is a different set of instincts from a GM in Sydney or Sheffield, and she has no idea that matters to a software company on the other side of the world. It should.

## A day in the life

A Friday.

- **09:30**: Walks the building. Checks the ice machines first, both of them, because the restaurant goes through close to a tonne of ice on a hot Friday and a dead ice machine is a dead bar.
- **10:30**: Office. Reviews last night's tip pool before the payroll cutoff: card tips by server, bar and busser tip-out percentages, and one dispute from a server who says her patio section transfer dropped her tips. Dee traces it, fixes it, and messages her before she comes in angry.
- **11:15**: Checks the roster against Texas rules she carries in her head: the 17-year-old busser cannot serve alcohol, and every bartender on tonight needs their TABC certification current. One expires next week. She flags it.
- **11:45**: Doors open. Lunch builds fast. She works the floor.
- **14:30**: Aggregator hour. DoorDash and Uber Eats between them are nearly a fifth of sales. A tablet lost sync during lunch and the kitchen remade an order twice. She logs the dispute and reminds herself, again, to ask why these do not just flow into the POS.
- **16:00**: Pre-shift with 30-odd staff. Eighty-sixed items, the game-day crowd expected at 18:00, and a reminder about comps: managers only, reason required, because the owners read the comp report.
- **18:45**: Full waitlist, 45-minute quote, every terminal running, pay-at-table devices out on the patio. A guest questions their receipt: menu price, 8.25 per cent sales tax, suggested tip amounts. Standard. She explains it in ten seconds and moves on.
- **22:30**: Close-out. Sales versus last Friday, labour percentage against the 28 per cent the owners want, card batch settlement, tip declarations. One server's declared cash tips look light against her card tips. Dee makes a note. The IRS cares about that pattern even when the server thinks nobody does.
- **23:30**: Lights off. Saturday will be bigger.

## Goals

- Beat last year's sales every week without letting labour percentage creep over target.
- A tip pool the staff trust completely: transparent, on time, correct to the cent, every pay cycle.
- Keep her best servers and bartenders in a city where every restaurant on the street is hiring.
- Turn tables faster on peak nights without guests feeling rushed, because in her economics, throughput is the whole game.
- Keep every inspection clean: health department, TABC, and the labour rules that come with a mostly tipped, partly under-21 workforce.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- Tip management is the sharpest edge in her week: pooling across roles and shifts, tip-outs to bar and bussers, card tips versus declared cash tips, and payroll reporting that has to stand up to an IRS look. Most software treats this as an afterthought. For her it is payroll, morale, and compliance in one.
- Any system that assumes tax is inside the price. Her menu is pre-tax, the register adds 8.25 per cent at the end, and tax-inclusive assumptions break receipts, reporting, and guest trust in one move.
- Delivery aggregators are a fifth of revenue and behave like a second restaurant: separate tablets, separate menus, separate disputes, commission she can recite from memory.
- Labour compliance that changes at the state line. Tipped minimum wage, tip credits, minors and alcohol, TABC certification. A product built for one country's rules, or even one state's, does not survive contact.
- High-volume nights expose everything: a terminal that queues at 19:30 on a Friday, a KDS that lags when 60 tickets are open, a payments batch that hiccups at close.
- Support that works Australian hours. Her 19:30 Friday crunch needs someone awake in her time zone.

---

## KPIs they are judged on

- Weekly sales versus forecast and versus last year.
- Labour cost as a percentage of sales, managed shift by shift.
- Food and beverage cost percentages.
- Table turn times and covers per service on peak nights.
- Guest ratings (Google, Yelp) and complaint patterns.
- Tip pool disputes and payroll accuracy, the informal KPI her staff judge her on.
- Health inspection score and alcohol compliance record.

## Tech profile

- **Devices in their hand**: Her phone, a manager terminal, pay-at-table handhelds during service, aggregator tablets she wishes did not exist, and the KDS screens she watches from the pass.
- **Current stack**: A US POS with integrated card payments, pay-at-table devices, a KDS, a waitlist and reservations app, a scheduling and tips platform, a payroll provider, DoorDash and Uber Eats, and QuickBooks at the owners' accountant.
- **Comfort level**: Functional, trending Deliberate. She will master anything that makes Friday easier and ignore everything else.
- **What she will not tolerate**: Latency at peak, tip maths she has to defend without evidence, and any surprise on a guest's receipt.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Operational spend, hires below assistant manager, supplier choices within the owners' framework, and small tools that do not touch money.
- **Influences**: POS, payments, scheduling, tips, reservations, and delivery decisions. The owners sign the contract, but they will not pick against her, because she runs the building.
- **Cannot touch**: The lease, capital works, the concept, and anything the investor partner considers a financial decision.
- **Who she needs on side**: The owners (signature), the kitchen manager (nothing works if the kitchen resists), and her senior servers (the tip pool's jury).

## What they need from Oolio

- Tips as a first-class system: pooling and tip-out rules by role and shift, card tips flowing to payroll cleanly, per-employee reporting that survives a dispute and an audit.
- US-shaped tax handling: pre-tax menu pricing, state and local sales tax applied at the register, and reporting her accountant recognises.
- Payments built for card-first, tip-on-card America: fast pay-at-table, obvious tip prompts, clean batch settlement at close.
- Delivery aggregator orders flowing straight into the POS and kitchen, with reconciliation she can hand to the owners' accountant.
- Peak-volume performance proven at her scale, with support awake in US hours when it is not.
- Labour and compliance awareness that respects state differences rather than hard-coding one country's rules.

---

## What loses them

- VAT-shaped or tax-inclusive assumptions anywhere: pricing, receipts, or reports.
- Tips treated as a bolt-on, a service charge, or somebody else's problem.
- A demo that has clearly never seen a thousand-cover night.
- Support hours that end when her Friday begins.
- Terminology that does not translate: her world is checks, servers, and sales tax, and a product that speaks only in dockets and GST reads as not-for-here.

---

## Strategic signal

Dee is the persona that tests whether Oolio travels. The United States is a named Oolio geography and the largest hospitality market in the world, but it runs on different physics: tipping as the wage system, sales tax added at the register, card-first payments, aggregator dominance, and state-by-state compliance. Every one of those is an assumption an AU-born product can silently get wrong. Dee exists so that any feature touching payments, tax, tips, receipts, or reporting is tested against a US Friday night before it ships to one. She is also the template for the US persona set to come; if the segmentation grid grows a US column, she is its anchor.
