# Liam Henderson

> Runs a Wetherspoons-scale pub. Lives by the head office spreadsheet.

---

## Snapshot

- **Role**: General Manager, The King's Arms (a venue inside a large UK pub group, ~620 sites)
- **Age**: 38
- **Location**: Birmingham city centre, United Kingdom
- **Venue**: A two-floor city-centre pub. 320 internal covers, a 90-cover beer garden, 14 hotel rooms above. Trades 09:00 to late, seven days a week.
- **Reports to**: The Area Operations Manager (one of ~40 across the estate).
- **Manages**: ~55 staff across FOH, kitchen, and housekeeping. Two assistant managers, four shift leaders, the rest hourly.

### Segmentation

- **Size and ownership**: Enterprise chain, 1 of 620 venues
- **Service style**: Pub and hotel (multi-revenue centre)
- **Geography**: United Kingdom, Birmingham
- **Tech maturity**: Functional (he uses what head office gives him, well, but he does not choose it)
- **Buying motion**: Procurement-led (he is a user, not a buyer)

### Oolio fit

- **Brand they would be on today**: bepoz at venue level for an enterprise pub group with gaming, accommodation, and food. In a procurement-led environment this would be the head office's choice, not his.
- **Solutions they touch**: POS, payments, KDS, stock and ordering (centrally controlled), labour scheduling, loyalty (centrally controlled), basic reporting.

---

## Bio

Liam started as a bar back at 18, worked through bar shifts, became a shift leader at 23, an assistant manager at 26, a deputy at 29, a GM at 33. He has been at this pub for four years, his second GM role. He runs a tight venue, consistently top-quartile in the region on margin and audit.

He is not paid to think strategically. He is paid to deliver the venue's numbers each week against a head-office plan. He is good at it. The trade-off is that he is at the bottom of a long buying chain. He does not pick the POS. He does not pick the supplier. He does not pick the menu. He executes.

His ambition is Area Operations Manager, then Regional Director. He sees how the GMs who get promoted operate. He copies it.

---

## A day in the life

A Saturday.

- **08:30**: Arrives. Reviews yesterday's takings against forecast on the head office portal. He is 4% ahead. He flags this in the WhatsApp group with the Area Ops Manager.
- **09:30**: Pre-shift huddle with the deputy. Five staff in today, two short. He calls a third on standby.
- **10:30**: Walks the venue. Checks the cellar gas, the gaming machines, the kitchen prep, the hotel rooms. Logs three small issues in the venue audit app.
- **12:00**: Lunch service. He covers a section because they are short. He is on the POS most of the lunch.
- **14:00**: Sits with the food deliveries. Reconciles them against the central ordering system. Two cases short. Raises it.
- **16:00**: Reads a head office bulletin about a new menu launch in three weeks. New SKUs, new pricing, new training. Plans how to roll it out.
- **18:00**: Evening service starts. Live football on. The pub fills. The KDS in the kitchen freezes for 90 seconds during the half-time rush. The kitchen cope. He logs the incident.
- **22:30**: Last orders. Counts the till floats. Reconciles cash to POS. Submits the daily takings to the head office portal.
- **00:30**: Locks up. Drives home.

## Goals

- Hit the weekly venue plan on revenue, GP%, wage%, and audit score.
- Stay in the top quartile of the region.
- Develop one of his deputies into a GM role within 12 months.
- Get promoted to Area Operations Manager within three years.
- Run a venue where the staff want to stay.

---

## Frustrations and pain points

- The POS is what it is. He does not choose it. When it breaks at peak, he loses revenue and explains it later.
- Head office reporting is high quality at group level and weak at venue level. He often has to recalculate venue-specific numbers from raw extracts.
- Menu launches are scripted to the minute. Anything that drifts from the script gets noticed.
- KDS reliability matters more than head office understands. A 90-second freeze on a Saturday is a real revenue and complaint event.
- He spends two evenings a week on admin that he believes the system should handle.
- The audit app, the portal, the labour tool, the POS, the comms, the bulletin board are all separate logins. He keeps a list.

---

## KPIs he is judged on

- Weekly net revenue versus venue plan.
- GP% versus plan.
- Wage% versus plan.
- Audit score (a head office compliance score across cleanliness, safety, training, brand standards).
- Customer feedback (NPS-style, central platform).
- Staff retention and training completion.

## Tech profile

- **Devices in his hand**: Corporate Android phone, a venue tablet at the bar, the POS terminals at the bar, a back-office PC for admin.
- **Current stack**: Enterprise POS (centrally chosen), enterprise KDS, head office labour scheduling tool, head office stock and ordering, head office BI portal, audit app, comms platform.
- **Comfort level**: Functional. He uses what he is given, gets fluent in it, and works around the gaps.
- **What he will not tolerate**: A POS that costs him a peak hour. He cannot tolerate it, because his Area Ops Manager will not tolerate it.

---

## Decision-making power

- **Can sign for**: Operational decisions within head-office framework. Casual hires. Local supplier requests within agreed lists. Local marketing within agreed brand standards.
- **Influences**: Head-office decisions, indirectly. If 40 GMs flag the same problem, head office listens. If one GM flags it, it does not move.
- **Cannot touch**: POS choice, payments choice, supplier list, menu, pricing, brand standards.
- **Who he needs on side**: His Area Operations Manager (everything escalates through him). His deputy GMs (operational delivery). His head chef (kitchen output).

## What he needs from Oolio

- A POS that is invisible at peak. No more than that.
- KDS that does not freeze.
- Reporting that gives him a clean venue view without him having to rebuild it.
- A roll-out experience that respects his venue's trading day. No cutovers on a Friday.
- A training package his hourly team can actually do in their paid training time.
- An incident process where Oolio talks to head office, not him, when something goes wrong.

---

## What loses him

- A peak-hour outage with a slow response.
- A roll-out that adds clicks at the till.
- A vendor who pitches to him directly, going around head office.
- A KDS that drops orders silently.
- Anything that adds to his admin tail.
