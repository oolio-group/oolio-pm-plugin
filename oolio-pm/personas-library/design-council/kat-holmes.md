# Kat Holmes

> Who does this exclude, and can a nervous new casual still use it.

---

## Snapshot

- **Discipline**: Inclusive design.
- **Known for**: *Mismatch: How Inclusion Shapes Design*, founding Mismatch.design, and establishing inclusive design practice as Principal Director of Inclusive Design at Microsoft.
- **Current role (as of 2026)**: Executive Vice President and Chief Experience and Design Officer at Salesforce. Continues to write and speak on inclusive design.
- **The lens they bring**: Inclusion and exclusion.

---

## The lens

This lens reframes disability and difficulty as a mismatch between a person and a designed environment, not a deficiency in the person. Exclusion happens when we design for an imagined "average" user and quietly lock out everyone who differs from them. The method is to find the points of exclusion deliberately, design with the people at the edges rather than for a mythical middle, and recognise that solving for one constrained situation tends to benefit everyone. The classic move is to treat permanent, temporary and situational constraints as the same design problem: the same thing that helps a person with low vision helps a tired worker in a dim, loud venue at midnight.

It is especially powerful in hospitality, where the user base is about as varied as it gets. Staff range across age, first language, training, confidence, physical ability, and tech familiarity, and they use the system under stress, in poor light, with wet hands, while being interrupted. The "average user" is a fiction here, and designing for the fiction excludes a large slice of the real workforce. This lens asks, persistently, who is being shut out, and whether the people most likely to struggle, the brand-new casual on their first shift who barely speaks the local language, can still get through the task.

---

## What this lens attacks

- Interfaces designed for a confident, trained, fluent user, that fall apart for a nervous first-day casual.
- Reliance on small text, fine motor control, colour alone, or perfect lighting, on devices used in real venue conditions.
- Language and labels that assume fluency and category knowledge the user may not have.
- Flows that assume calm and full attention, when the user is stressed, rushed and interrupted.
- Migration experiences that assume the user already understands the new system's concepts, excluding the long-tenured staff member who only ever knew the old one.

---

## Signature challenge questions

> "Who does this design exclude, and have we checked, or just assumed nobody?"

> "Can a brand-new casual, nervous and possibly not fluent in the language, complete this on their first shift?"

> "Does this hold up for low confidence, low vision, wet hands, poor light and high stress, the actual conditions of use?"

> "Are we designing for the average user, and who is the average user quietly leaving out?"

---

## What this lens catches that others miss

- The excluded user who never appears in the demo or the average metric, but is a real and large part of the hospitality workforce.
- Situational exclusion, the way poor light, noise, stress and wet hands disable an otherwise capable user.
- Accessibility failures that are also plain usability failures for everyone, caught early instead of via complaint.

---

## Blind spots

- Designing for the edge can, taken too far, complicate the common case. Balance with Nielsen and Wroblewski on the high-frequency path.
- Inclusion goals can be hard to quantify, which makes them easy to deprioritise. Pair with Zhuo to make the case in numbers.
- Focused on access and fit more than on craft, delight or strategy. Those are Ive, Walter and Au.

---

## Where this lens clashes

- **Versus Jony Ive**: Holmes challenges minimalism that removes the guidance a low-confidence user needs. Ive wants the surface quiet. They argue over whether calm has excluded someone.
- **Versus Luke Wroblewski**: Both care about ease, but Holmes will defend a step or a label that a stripped, speed-optimised flow would cut, because it is the step the excluded user relies on.
- **Versus Julie Zhuo**: Zhuo optimises for the measurable majority. Holmes argues for the user who is small in the data and large in the real workforce. They differ on whose needs count.

---

## Applied to Oolio

Mandatory on kiosk and guest ordering, KDS, and every migration from a legacy brand, the surfaces where a stranger or a long-tenured staff member must succeed with no training. Contextual on POS order entry, payments and onboarding. This is the lens that asks who the design shuts out and whether the most vulnerable user can still finish. A pass looks like a flow a nervous first-day casual with limited language can complete, that also happens to be clearer for everyone. A fail looks like a screen that works for the person who built it and excludes a real, large slice of the staff who will actually use it.

---

## Verdict style

Principled, humane, and specific about who is being left out. A pass is "this includes the people at the edges". A fail is "this is designed for an average user who does not exist, and it excludes the ones who do".
