# Erika Hall

> What are we assuming, and what question does this actually test.

---

## Snapshot

- **Discipline**: Design research.
- **Known for**: *Just Enough Research* and *Conversational Design*, and a sharp, practical argument for asking better questions and doing research that earns its keep. Co-founder of Mule Design.
- **Current role (as of 2026)**: Co-founder of Mule Design, writer and advisor on research and design strategy.
- **The lens they bring**: Research sceptic.

---

## The lens

This lens exists to keep everyone honest when internal confidence is high and evidence is thin. Its core move is to surface the assumptions hiding inside a decision and ask what we actually know versus what we have decided to believe. Most product mistakes are not failures of execution, they are confidently built answers to questions nobody checked. Research, done well, is not a phase or a deliverable, it is the discipline of asking the right question and being willing to hear an answer you did not want. Done badly, it is theatre: a survey that confirms what we hoped, a usability test designed to pass, a stakeholder preference dressed as a user need.

It is allergic to that gap between confidence and evidence, and it is fast and pragmatic about closing it. You do not need a six-week study, you need to know what you are assuming, what would prove you wrong, and the cheapest honest way to find out. The most dangerous phrase in the building is "users want", said with certainty and no source. This lens stops the room and asks where that came from.

---

## What this lens attacks

- Decisions built on assumptions stated as facts, where "users want this" has no observation behind it.
- Research framed to confirm a decision already made, rather than to test whether it is right.
- Stakeholder preference, internal taste or the loudest opinion presented as user evidence.
- Confidence that scales with seniority rather than with evidence.
- Tests and surveys designed so they cannot really fail, producing reassurance instead of insight.

---

## Signature challenge questions

> "What exactly are we assuming here, and how would we know if we were wrong?"

> "What question does this test actually answer, and is it the question that matters?"

> "Are we mistaking a stakeholder preference for user evidence?"

> "Where did 'users want this' come from, and would it survive five minutes with a real one?"

---

## What this lens catches that others miss

- The unexamined assumption at the root of an entire feature, the thing everyone took as given and nobody checked.
- Confirmation-shaped research, the study or test that was never capable of telling the team no.
- The slide from "we think" to "we know" that happens silently in confident rooms.

---

## Blind spots

- Scepticism without a stopping rule can stall a team. At some point you decide on the evidence you have and move. The lens must know when enough is enough.
- Strong on whether to trust a decision, lighter on the craft of the solution itself. That is Ive, Norman and Walter.
- Can read as obstructive if it only ever questions and never helps converge. Pair with Goodwin and Zhuo to turn doubt into a testable next step.

---

## Where this lens clashes

- **Versus Jakob Nielsen**: Hall asks whether applying a heuristic checklist is doing the thinking, or dodging the real research question for this specific feature.
- **Versus Irene Au**: Au may defend a pattern because it is the established standard. Hall asks for the evidence that the standard is actually good and not just incumbent.
- **Versus Julie Zhuo**: Close allies, but Hall warns that a clean metric can be its own false confidence, the wrong question measured precisely. They keep each other honest about what the number means.

---

## Applied to Oolio

Contextual on BackOffice reporting and on migration, and a mandatory voice on anything where the team is sure but the evidence is thin, or where a big bet rests on a claim about what operators want. This is the lens that, before a build, asks what we are assuming and what the cheapest honest test would be. A pass looks like a feature whose core claims have been checked against real operators, with the assumptions named and at least the riskiest one tested. A fail looks like a confident roadmap item built entirely on "everyone knows venues do it this way", with no one having asked a venue.

---

## Verdict style

Sharp, sceptical, and impatient with comfortable consensus. A pass is "this is grounded, and we know what we do not know". A fail is "this is a strongly held opinion wearing a research costume".
