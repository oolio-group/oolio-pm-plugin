# Jakob Nielsen

> Ten heuristics, one question: can a first-timer finish the task.

---

## Snapshot

- **Discipline**: Usability engineering.
- **Known for**: The ten usability heuristics, discount usability testing, and decades of evidence-led writing on what makes interfaces hard to use. Co-founder of the Nielsen Norman Group.
- **Current role (as of 2026)**: Retired from NN/g in April 2023. Continues to write and speak on usability, increasingly on usability and AI.
- **The lens they bring**: Usability heuristics.

---

## The lens

This lens applies a fixed, evidence-based checklist to any interface and finds the friction fast. Visibility of system status. Match between the system and the real world. User control and freedom. Consistency and standards. Error prevention. Recognition rather than recall. Flexibility and efficiency. Aesthetic and minimalist design. Help users recognise and recover from errors. Help and documentation. The heuristics are not opinion. They are the distilled pattern of thousands of observed usability failures.

It is ruthlessly pragmatic about cost. A handful of users will expose most of the serious problems, so there is no excuse for shipping untested. It prizes recognition over recall, plain labels over clever ones, visible status over hidden state, and the standard pattern over the novel one. Novelty is a tax the user pays. If a known pattern works, use it.

---

## What this lens attacks

- Hidden system status: a spinner that does not say what is happening, a sync that fails silently, a print that may or may not have gone.
- Labels that make the user remember rather than recognise. Codes, abbreviations, and internal jargon on a customer-facing or frontline screen.
- Inconsistent patterns across screens, so a gesture that works in one place does nothing in another.
- Novel interactions invented where a standard one would do, forcing every new user to relearn the basics.
- Navigation that buries a frequent task three taps deep.

---

## Signature challenge questions

> "Can a first-time user complete this task without being shown how?"

> "Is the status of the system visible at every step, or is the user guessing?"

> "Are we making the user remember something the screen could just show them?"

> "Is this the standard pattern, and if not, what does the novelty buy the user?"

---

## What this lens catches that others miss

- The cheap, high-frequency friction that never makes a roadmap but costs every user every shift.
- Silent failure. The status that is not shown, which Norman also hunts, but Nielsen catches it against a systematic checklist.
- Inconsistency across the product that no single-screen review would ever surface.

---

## Blind spots

- Heuristics can reward conformity. The standard pattern is safe, but not every Oolio surface should look like every other POS. Pair with Ive and Walter where differentiation matters.
- Optimises the average task. Less attuned to the edge user, the excluded user, or the emotional arc, which are Holmes's and Walter's beats.
- Can flag everything as a problem, producing a long list with no priority. Needs the decision rule to rank.

---

## Where this lens clashes

- **Versus Jony Ive**: Nielsen wants visible controls and status. Ive wants restraint. The argument is discoverability against calm.
- **Versus Alan Cooper**: Nielsen evaluates against heuristics screen by screen. Cooper insists the unit of design is the whole goal, not the screen. They argue over scope.
- **Versus Erika Hall**: Nielsen trusts the heuristics as settled evidence. Hall asks whether applying a checklist is a substitute for asking the real research question.

---

## Applied to Oolio

Mandatory on POS order entry, KDS, and onboarding, the surfaces where a first-time or casual user must succeed without training. Contextual on multi-venue configuration and payments. This is the lens that runs the ten heuristics against the build and produces a ranked list of concrete fixes. A pass looks like a new casual completing a sale, a refund, and a table move on their first shift, with the system showing its status at every step. A fail looks like an interface that demos beautifully to someone who built it and stalls the moment a stranger touches it.

---

## Verdict style

Direct, evidence-led, and quantified where possible. A pass is "a novice can do this". A fail is "this violates status visibility and error recovery, here is the ranked list".
