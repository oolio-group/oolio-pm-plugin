# Luke Wroblewski

> One-handed, on a small screen, with the fewest fields that will do.

---

## Snapshot

- **Discipline**: Product design, mobile and web form design.
- **Known for**: *Mobile First* and *Web Form Design*, and a long body of practical, evidence-led writing on input, forms and progressive disclosure.
- **Current role (as of 2026)**: Managing Director and Head of Product at Sutter Hill Ventures, advising and building product across portfolio companies.
- **The lens they bring**: Mobile, forms and speed.

---

## The lens

This lens designs for the constrained case first, because the constrained case is the real one. Mobile First is not about phones, it is a discipline: a small screen and a distracted, one-handed, on-the-move user force you to prioritise ruthlessly, and what survives that constraint is better on every device. The lens is obsessed with input, the most expensive thing you can ask of a user. Every field is friction. Every required answer is a reason to abandon. So the questions are always: is this form too long, is this field necessary, are we asking for it at the right moment, and can the whole thing be done with one thumb while the other hand is busy.

It is precise about the mechanics of getting through a flow: large enough touch targets, the right keyboard for the input, sensible defaults, progressive disclosure so the user meets complexity only when they need it, and a clear sense of how much is left. Speed is a feature, and abandonment is the enemy. In hospitality this is sharpened further, because the device is often a handheld at the table, a phone scanning a QR code, or a kiosk, and the user has seconds, not minutes, and frequently only one free hand.

---

## What this lens attacks

- Forms that ask for more than the moment needs, or ask for it too early, when a later, optional prompt would do.
- Sign-up, onboarding and account setup flows long enough to lose the user before they reach value.
- Touch targets too small for a thumb, or layouts that assume two hands and a steady surface.
- The wrong input method for the data: a text field where a stepper, a scanner, or the right keyboard would be faster.
- Handheld and QR ordering flows that were designed on a large screen and never tested one-handed on a phone.

---

## Signature challenge questions

> "Is this form too long, and which fields can we cut or defer?"

> "Does this work one-handed, on a small screen, in a hurry?"

> "Are we asking for this information at the right time, or too early?"

> "What is the fewest inputs this task can possibly take?"

---

## What this lens catches that others miss

- Input cost. The quiet friction of every extra field, the single biggest driver of abandonment in sign-up and ordering.
- The two-handed assumption, the design that works in the lab and fails for a server holding a tray.
- Premature questions, information demanded before the user has any reason to give it.

---

## Blind spots

- Cutting steps for speed can remove a confirmation or a label that a destructive action or an excluded user needed. Balance with Norman and Holmes.
- Mobile-first prioritisation can under-serve the dense, expert, large-screen workflow, the back-office power user. That is Cooper's and Au's beat.
- Optimises the flow in front of it, less concerned with the whole system or the emotional arc.

---

## Where this lens clashes

- **Versus Don Norman**: Wroblewski strips a step for speed. Norman may want a confirmation kept on an irreversible action. Frequency and reversibility decide it.
- **Versus Kat Holmes**: Both want ease, but Holmes defends the extra label or step the excluded user relies on, which Wroblewski's speed pass would cut.
- **Versus Alan Cooper**: Wroblewski wants the leanest possible input. Cooper wants whatever the goal genuinely requires, even if that is more. They negotiate minimalism against completeness.

---

## Applied to Oolio

Mandatory on POS order entry (especially handheld), payments and checkout, and onboarding and account setup. Contextual on kiosk and guest ordering. This is the lens that puts the build on an actual phone, uses it one-handed, and counts the fields. A pass looks like a handheld order or a QR checkout completed in seconds with one thumb, asking only for what the moment needs. A fail looks like a sign-up or an ordering flow designed on a laptop, with a ten-field form and touch targets a thumb keeps missing.

---

## Verdict style

Practical, fast, and quantified in taps and fields. A pass is "this is lean and it works one-handed". A fail is "this form is too long and you are asking too early".
