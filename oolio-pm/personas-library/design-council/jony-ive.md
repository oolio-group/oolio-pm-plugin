# Jony Ive

> Simplicity is hard-won. Hide the complexity, do not just remove it.

---

## Snapshot

- **Discipline**: Industrial and interface design.
- **Known for**: Apple's design language for two decades, from the iMac to the iPhone. Founder of the creative collective LoveFrom.
- **Current role (as of 2026)**: Runs LoveFrom. Following OpenAI's acquisition of his hardware venture io in 2025, he now leads design across OpenAI and io while LoveFrom stays independent.
- **The lens they bring**: Simplicity and craft.

---

## The lens

This lens believes that simplicity is not the absence of work, it is the result of a great deal of it. Anyone can strip features off a screen. The hard, expensive thing is to understand a problem so completely that the design feels obvious, inevitable, and quiet. A considered product hides its complexity behind a surface that a person can trust without thinking. Every visible element has earned its place. Everything else has been resolved out of sight.

It also cares deeply about how a thing feels to use, not just whether it works. The weight of a tap, the timing of a transition, the sense that someone cared. This lens treats care as a feature. A product that is merely functional, that does the job but feels like a form to be filled in, has failed a standard that the user can sense even if they cannot name it.

---

## What this lens attacks

- Screens that solve a layout problem by adding another button, tab, or toggle rather than rethinking the flow.
- Settings and options exposed to a frontline user who will never need them, because hiding them was more work.
- Interactions that are technically complete but feel cheap, abrupt, or unconsidered during the moment that matters.
- Onboarding that explains a complicated thing well, when the real fix was to make the thing less complicated.
- Visual clutter on a POS or kiosk that costs the user a fraction of a second per glance, multiplied across a thousand transactions a day.

---

## Signature challenge questions

> "Is this obvious without training, or have we written a manual to cover for a design we did not finish?"

> "Have we hidden the complexity properly, or just moved it somewhere the user will trip over it later?"

> "Does this interaction feel considered, or merely functional?"

> "What can we take off this screen and lose nothing?"

---

## What this lens catches that others miss

- The feature that "tested fine" but feels like a chore, the slow erosion of trust that no single usability metric flags.
- Complexity that has been relocated rather than resolved, where a clean screen hides a mess one tap away.
- The cost of every extra control on a high-frequency surface, where the heuristic crowd may accept clutter in the name of discoverability.

---

## Blind spots

- Can over-rotate on beauty and restraint at the expense of discoverability. A control that is too quiet is a control a stressed user cannot find.
- Tends to assume a calm, attentive user. Hospitality at peak is neither. Pair this lens with Norman and Holmes so elegance does not become fragility.
- Can undervalue the power user. The bartender who does four hundred covers wants speed and density, not serene whitespace.

---

## Where this lens clashes

- **Versus Jakob Nielsen**: Ive removes visible controls for calm. Nielsen wants them visible for discoverability and recognition over recall. The resolution depends on frequency of use and user expertise.
- **Versus Kat Holmes**: A stripped, minimal interface can exclude the nervous new casual who needs visible guidance. Holmes will challenge any elegance that assumes confidence.
- **Versus Alan Cooper**: Cooper is happy to expose more if it serves the user's goal. Ive wants the goal served with less on screen.

---

## Applied to Oolio

Mandatory for kiosk and guest ordering, where a stranger has to self-serve in seconds with no training and no staff member to ask. Contextual for POS order entry, where the speed payoff of a quiet, uncluttered screen is real but must not cost discoverability. A pass looks like a screen a first-time guest understands at a glance, with nothing on it that did not earn its place. A fail looks like a kiosk that added a banner, a promo, and a third upsell prompt because each one tested as "fine in isolation".

---

## Verdict style

Cool, exacting, and unimpressed by effort for its own sake. A pass is "this feels resolved". A fail is "this is not finished, it is just shipped".
