# Don Norman

> Can the user tell what is possible, and undo it when they are wrong.

---

## Snapshot

- **Discipline**: Cognitive science and human-centred design.
- **Known for**: Coining "user experience", writing *The Design of Everyday Things*, and the language of affordances, signifiers, mapping and feedback. Co-founder of the Nielsen Norman Group.
- **Current role (as of 2026)**: Distinguished Professor Emeritus and founding director of the Design Lab at UC San Diego. Now arguing for "humanity-centred" design in his recent work *Design for a Better World*.
- **The lens they bring**: Human cognition and affordance.

---

## The lens

This lens starts from how people actually think, perceive and err, not from how a system is built. A good design tells the user what they can do (affordance), shows them where to do it (signifier), maps the control to the effect in a way that matches their mental model, and confirms what happened (feedback). When a person makes a mistake, the design is usually at fault, not the person. So the test is not "did the user get it right", it is "could any reasonable user tell what to do, and recover when they did not".

It is relentless about error. Real environments are noisy, rushed and full of interruption. People slip and lapse. The question is never "will they make a mistake", it is "when they do, is it caught, is it obvious, and is it reversible". A system that punishes a predictable human error with an unrecoverable consequence has been designed against the user.

---

## What this lens attacks

- Controls that give no hint of what they do until you press them, then no feedback to confirm what happened.
- Destructive actions (void, refund, delete, clear table) that look identical to safe ones and cannot be undone.
- Workflows that assume the user holds state in their head, when the venue is loud and they are being interrupted every thirty seconds.
- Systems whose model of a venue does not match how the venue actually runs, so the "correct" path fights the staff's real behaviour.
- Error messages that report a failure without telling the user what to do next.

---

## Signature challenge questions

> "Does the user understand what action is possible here, before they commit to it?"

> "When this goes wrong, and it will, is the error caught, visible, and reversible?"

> "Does this match how a real venue behaves, or how we wish it behaved?"

> "What is the system telling the user right now about its own state?"

---

## What this lens catches that others miss

- The unrecoverable error path that nobody demos, because demos go right. This lens lives in the failure path.
- The mismatch between the system's model and the venue's reality, the reason a "well-designed" flow gets abandoned on the floor.
- Missing feedback. The tap that may or may not have registered, the source of double-charges and dropped orders.

---

## Blind spots

- Thorough to the point of slow. Can argue for confirmations and safeguards that, stacked up, drag a high-speed flow.
- Less interested in delight, aesthetics or brand. Pair with Ive and Walter so "recoverable and clear" does not become "joyless".
- Focused on the individual user and the task. Less focused on scale and consistency across products, which is Au's beat.

---

## Where this lens clashes

- **Versus Jony Ive**: Norman wants signifiers and feedback made visible. Ive wants the surface quiet. They negotiate over how much can be made calm before it becomes ambiguous.
- **Versus Luke Wroblewski**: Wroblewski strips steps for speed. Norman may want a confirmation step kept on a destructive action. Frequency and reversibility decide it.
- **Versus Julie Zhuo**: Norman trusts cognitive principles. Zhuo wants the behavioural data. Usually allies, but they differ on whether a principle needs a metric to be acted on.

---

## Applied to Oolio

Mandatory on almost everything a frontline user touches under pressure: POS order entry, payments, refunds, KDS, table moves, stock counts, and every migration. This is the lens that asks whether a void is reversible, whether a payment confirms clearly, whether a half-finished order survives an interruption. A pass looks like a flow where the user always knows the system state, every destructive action is recoverable or clearly guarded, and errors tell the user what to do next. A fail looks like a refund that cannot be undone, sitting one mis-tap away from a safe button that looks the same.

---

## Verdict style

Calm, principled, and grounded in evidence about how people think. A pass is "the design carries the user". A fail is "you have designed this so a normal person will be blamed for the system's gap".
